EXPERT REVIEW MMM FRAMEWORK · v0.2.0 LENSES: STATISTICIAN · PLANNER · CMO 2026·07·11

The gap between rigorous and adopted

The Last Mile:
making a rigorous model trusted.

This platform is already in the methodological top tier — a standalone Bayesian engine with pre-registration, simulation-based calibration, experiment calibration, EIG/EVOI experiment design, and genuinely honest uncertainty. So the remaining work is not more Bayesian machinery. It is the last mile: causal-validity guardrails, fitting how planners actually work, and letting a CMO defend the number to a CFO.

Credit where it's due — the critique below assumes all of this already works

Honesty

Causal

01

The statistician's lens

Is the number identified & causal?

Your validation stack proves the model samples correctly and predicts well. Neither of those is the same as proving a channel's ROI is its true incremental effect. Every issue below is about closing that specific gap — and about refusing to let a prior masquerade as a finding.

S1Identification

Weakly-identified individual ROIs behind a well-fit total

TodayAdstock × saturation × collinear, always-on channels make per-channel effects fragile even when total contribution is solid. You already compute contraction diagnostics and an identification module.

GapMake identifiability a hard gate on every reported number. If a channel's posterior barely moved off its prior, or it's collinear with another, the client-facing ROI should say so — even print "we cannot separate Search and Shopping in this data" rather than two confident, invented numbers.

S2Spec search

Specification uncertainty — the garden of forking paths

TodayLOO/WAIC exist per model. But there are dozens of defensible specs (adstock form, saturation form, control set, pooling) and nothing stops an analyst from landing on the one that gives the "nice" answer.

GapA spec-curve / Bayesian model-average over a pre-registered set of specs. Report how each ROI moves across all defensible specifications, not a single hand-picked one. Robustness across specs is itself a headline result.

S3Endogeneity

Spend responds to expected demand

TodayThe latent-factor confounder model is exactly the right idea for the back-door spend ← demand → sales. But it needs confounder indicators (macro, seasonality proxies) that most clients don't have.

GapSurface the load-bearing assumption plainly: MMM ROI is causal only under no-unobserved-confounding. Add endogeneity diagnostics (does spend lead demand?) and route the honest answer to "confirm with an experiment."

S4Validation

Predictive accuracy ≠ causal validity

TodayBacktest MAPE, PPC and LOO-PIT show the model reproduces and forecasts the data. Experiment calibration is your one true causal anchor.

GapPromote "has this channel's effect ever been experimentally confirmed?" to a first-class, per-channel trust signal. You already track calibration coverage — make it the badge that outranks fit statistics in the client view.

S5Multiplicity

Many simultaneous estimands, treated independently

TodayChannels × geos × products yield dozens of ROIs at once. Hierarchical partial pooling shrinks them, which helps.

GapReport joint, shrinkage-aware uncertainty, not a grid of independent CIs. "Which of these effects are actually distinguishable from zero — and from each other?" is the question a statistician will ask, and independent intervals overstate how much you know.

S6Stationarity

Structural breaks inside the training window

TodaySynthetic trend-break worlds exist; the continuous-learning side has drift discounting.

GapA standard "is there a regime change in this window?" diagnostic (COVID, a pricing reset, a creative overhaul) with guidance, so a response curve fit across two different worlds is flagged before it's trusted.

02

The media planner's lens

Can I build & defend a plan from this?

Your optimizer, flighting calendar, and decision-value tools (regret, EVOI) are genuinely ahead of the market. The planner's objections are about the boundary of what MMM can see, extrapolation risk, and the daily rhythm of buying — the places where a model recommendation meets a media plan.

P1Resolution

From "allocation" to an actual media plan

TodayThe optimizer returns channel dollars and a flighting calendar; sub-channel share exists.

GapReach/frequency curves and creative/audience/daypart live below MMM resolution — say so, and fill them with experiment and platform data. A planner needs "at what frequency does this saturate," not just "spend $2M on Social."

P2Extrapolation

Recommendations that step outside observed spend

TodayResponse curves are estimated over roughly 0–2× historical spend; the optimizer can propose large moves.

GapHard extrapolation flags with widened uncertainty on any recommended spend beyond the observed range. "+150% on a channel we've only ever run at one level" is a guess dressed as a curve — mark it as such.

P3Confidence

Lead with decision confidence, not the allocation

TodayYou compute expected regret, EVOI and probability-of-improvement — better than most tools ship.

GapMake "78% chance this plan beats last year's" the headline of every recommendation, not a deep-dive metric. Planners defend decisions upward; hand them the confidence statement first.

P4In-flight

No pacing / actual-vs-plan loop

TodayMMM is retrospective; the measurement loop re-tests on a decay cadence.

GapClose the loop between the recommended plan and live delivery. Flag when actual spend diverges from plan, and what that does to the expected outcome. This is where planners live day-to-day, and it's currently a blind spot.

P5Context

Competitive activity / share of voice isn't a driver

TodayOwn-channel cannibalization/cross-effects are modeled; external competitor spend is not.

GapEither ingest competitive spend / SOV as a control (with an explicit confounding caveat) or scope it out loudly. Planners reason in SOV; a silent omission reads as a blind model.

P6Cadence

Match the answer's speed to the question

TodayWeekly MMM + NUTS is slow; approximate fits and the continuous-learning bandit exist for fast questions.

GapMake the "fast in-flight question vs slow strategic question" routing explicit, and default to the sub-channel taxonomy wherever the data supports it — planners will (rightly) reject "Social" as too coarse.

03

The CMO's lens

Can I defend this to the CFO & board?

The CMO carries the number into rooms where the audience is skeptical and speaks P&L, not posteriors. Your model-defense deck and augur readouts are strong raw material. The gaps are about translation to the business, long-term brand, and an accountability trail that survives contact with a CFO.

C1P&L

Speak the CFO's language

TodayROI in revenue, margin-aware net, counterfactual and opportunity-cost all exist as pieces.

GapA CFO-grade one-pager: total marketing contribution to the P&L, and "cut marketing X% → this much revenue/profit at risk, with uncertainty." The components are there; they need to roll up into one defensible statement.

C2Long-term

Weekly MMM under-credits brand

TodayAdstock carryover and the structural survey-funnel extension capture some persistence.

GapAn explicit short-term-activation vs long-term-brand treatment, with a caveat. Left unaddressed, MMM systematically over-rotates budget to performance channels and starves brand — the single most consequential way an MMM misleads a CMO.

C3Credibility

"Prove it" — the incrementality challenge

TodayThe whole T0→T5 loop (experiments → calibration) is the answer, and the model-defense deck is a real asset.

GapMake experiment-validated vs modeled the single most prominent label on every number the CMO presents. The CFO's trust is bought one confirmed channel at a time; show that ledger explicitly.

C4Benchmark

"Is 3× ROAS good?"

TodayNo external category benchmarks (credibly hard to source).

GapAt minimum, frame every result against internal trend and the break-even line so a number has context. A ROAS with no reference point invites the wrong argument.

C5Governance

Sign-off & audit trail

TodayVersioned assumptions and pre-registration are strong foundations.

GapFormalize a named approval step — "who signed off on these priors" — and an immutable record for the auditor. Enterprise adoption stalls without a clear accountability chain.

C6Accountability

Did last quarter's recommendation actually pay off?

TodayRun-history metrics and T5 re-evaluation persist the raw material.

GapA recommendation scorecard: predicted lift vs realized lift over time. Nothing builds a CMO's trust in a model faster than watching its past calls come true — or seeing it honestly own the misses.

04

Presenting results so they stay statistically sound

The client-facing discipline

The failure mode isn't bad math — it's a sound model communicated in a way that invites over-confident decisions. The remedy is a small set of non-negotiable presentation habits. Your interactive report already delivers several of these; the point is to make them the standard, not the option.

Experiment-validated Model-identified Prior-dominated

Adopt one visual language for evidence strength and apply it to every number, everywhere — the same three-way coding your augur readouts already hint at. A client should never have to ask "how much of this is data?"

01

Lead with the decision and its confidence, not the parameter

The audience acts on outcomes, not coefficients. Translate every finding into a move and a probability.

DO"Shift $2M from A to B → +$3.1M revenue (80% CI $0.4M–$6.0M); 78% chance it beats status quo."
NO"βB = 0.42, posterior sd 0.11."
02

Every number carries an interval and an evidence label

A point estimate with no uncertainty is a lie of omission; an interval with no provenance hides whether it's data or assumption. Show both, always.

03

Separate average ROI from marginal (next-dollar) ROI

Clients reallocate on the wrong metric constantly. Historical efficiency (average) and what the next dollar buys (marginal) diverge under saturation — and only the marginal one governs a budget move. Never let a plan optimize on average ROAS.

04

Put the causal assumptions on the same page as the numbers

No unobserved confounders; response curves stable over the horizon; experiment-validated where noted. Stated plainly and up front — not buried in an appendix — this is what makes the result honest rather than merely persuasive.

05

Decompose base vs incremental — and show how little marketing usually moves

Most sales are base. Showing that marketing drives a minority of the total, honestly, is what keeps the incremental claims credible when the CFO pressure-tests them.

06

Pre-register, and show that you did

"We committed to these channels, priors and estimands before seeing the results." Your pre-fit design readout is this — make it a client deliverable, because it pre-empts the p-hacking objection before it's raised.

07

Refuse false precision; reconcile with other evidence

A "3.24× ROAS" with a 1.1–6.2× interval should read "≈3×, wide uncertainty." And add a triangulation panel — MMM vs experiment vs platform-reported — with a reconciled recommendation and an explanation of any disagreement. Convergence across methods is the most persuasive slide you can show.

08

Let the client stress it themselves

Fan charts instead of single lines; response curves with the observed-spend range shaded and extrapolation greyed; PPC shown as "does the model reproduce reality." Your recompute-in-browser report already lets a client change the window and watch the number move — that interactivity is one of the strongest trust-builders you have. Lean into it.

Anatomy of a sound client recommendation

"Move $2.0M from Display to Connected TV. Expected gain +$3.1M revenue (80% CI $0.4M–$6.0M) · 78% chance it beats the current plan · CTV effect is experiment-validated, Display is model-identified only. Assumes no major demand shock and spend stays within tested range. Recommend a confirmatory geo test before committing beyond $1.2M."

Bottom line

The modeling is not the risk — this platform is already more rigorous than most of what ships in the category. The risk is a sophisticated buyer who can't tell which numbers to trust, a planner who can't turn an allocation into a plan, and a CMO who can't defend either to a CFO. The last mile is disclosure, translation, and accountability — mostly surfacing what the model already knows, in the language each of the three readers speaks.
