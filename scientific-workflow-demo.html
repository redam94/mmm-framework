1 Define the Question

What do you actually want to learn?

Every good analysis starts with a clear question. This might seem obvious, but skipping this step is the most common mistake in marketing measurement. Without a clear question, you end up with lots of numbers but no insights.

Think of it like planning a trip

You wouldn't open Google Maps before knowing where you want to go. The destination determines the route. Similarly, your business question determines what analysis to run, what data you need, and how to interpret the results.

Our Example Question

For this walkthrough: "What is the ROI of each marketing channel, and how should we allocate our budget?"

This is a causal question‚Äîwe want to know if changing our marketing spend will cause sales to change. That's harder than measuring correlation, but it's what we need for actual decisions.

Why we write this down before analyzing

When you decide your question after seeing the data, you can unconsciously twist the question to match whatever the data happened to show. That's like drawing a target around wherever your arrow landed‚Äîit looks impressive but doesn't mean you're a good archer.

This practice of deciding your analysis plan beforehand is called pre-registration, and it's one of the most important safeguards against fooling yourself.

What We're Committing To

  • Primary question: What is the ROI of each channel?
  • Secondary question: What's the optimal budget mix?
  • Channels to analyze: TV, Paid Search, Social, Display, Radio
  • Time period: 2 years of weekly data
  • Success metric: Sales revenue

2 Tell the Story

How do we think marketing actually drives sales?

Before any math, we need a story about how things work. This isn't just a formality‚Äîit's how we turn business knowledge into something a computer can work with. The story forces us to make our assumptions explicit, which means we can question them, test them, and communicate them to stakeholders.

Like writing a recipe

A recipe explains how ingredients combine to make a dish: "Take 2 cups flour, add 1 cup sugar, mix well, bake at 350¬∞." Our statistical story does the same thing for sales: "Start with baseline demand, add effects from each media channel, account for seasonality, include some randomness..."

The Story of Acme's Sales

Imagine you could watch Acme's revenue being generated week by week. What would you see? Let's build up the story piece by piece.

üì¶

Chapter 1: The Baseline

Even without any marketing, Acme would sell some products. People know the brand, stores carry it, there's natural demand from existing customers and word-of-mouth. This is our starting point‚Äîcall it around $15 million per week.

üì∫

Chapter 2: Marketing Lifts Sales

Each channel (TV, digital, etc.) adds sales above baseline. But here's the catch: the first million dollars of TV spend helps more than the tenth million. This is called diminishing returns‚Äîlike eating pizza slices, the first few are great but eventually you're full.

‚è∞

Chapter 3: Ads Have Lasting Effects

When you see a TV ad, you don't always buy immediately. You might wait until next week or next month. This "carryover" effect means today's advertising affects sales for several weeks. We call this adstock.

üéÑ

Chapter 4: Seasonality Matters

Sales go up around holidays and down in slow periods. This happens regardless of marketing‚Äîit's just how consumer behavior works. We need to account for this so we don't accidentally credit marketing for holiday bumps.

üé≤

Chapter 5: Random Variation

Even after accounting for everything, there's always some unexplained variation. A celebrity mentions your product, a competitor has a recall, weather affects shopping. We can't predict these, but we can acknowledge they exist.

Putting the story together

"Sales start from a baseline of existing demand. Marketing channels add to this, but with diminishing returns as spend increases. The effects carry over for several weeks as people remember and act on ads. Underlying all of this, sales rise and fall with natural seasonal patterns. And there's always some random variation we can't explain."

This story‚Äîthese assumptions‚Äîwill become our mathematical model. If we're wrong about any part of this story, our results will be wrong. That's why we test our assumptions later in the workflow.

4 Prior Predictive Check

Do our assumptions generate plausible predictions?

Before showing the model any real data, we test whether our assumptions could plausibly generate realistic-looking outcomes. This catches problems early‚Äîbefore we waste time fitting a broken model.

Like a dress rehearsal

Before opening night, actors do a full rehearsal to catch problems. We run our model "forward" without real data: given our assumptions about how marketing works, what kind of sales numbers would we expect to see?

What if predictions look wrong?

If the prior predictive shows impossible outcomes (negative sales, values in the trillions), it means our assumptions are too vague. Tighten the priors‚Äîadd more knowledge. This is good‚Äîwe caught a problem before wasting computation on fitting.

5 Fit the Model

Let the data update our beliefs

Now we show our model the actual sales data and let it figure out what the media effects probably are. This is the core of Bayesian inference: starting with prior beliefs, updating them with evidence.

Updating opinions with evidence

Imagine you think a coin is fair. Then you flip it 100 times and get 75 heads. You'd update your belief‚Äîmaybe it's biased. Our model does the same: it starts with prior beliefs about media effects and updates them based on the sales data.

7 Posterior Predictive Check

The algorithm worked (Step 6), but is the model any good? We check by asking: can this model reproduce data that looks like what we actually observed? If not, the model is missing something important.

Checking your understanding

If you truly understand how something works, you should be able to recreate it. If our model truly captures how sales are generated, it should produce realistic fake data that matches the patterns in real data.

Why sensitivity matters

If conclusions change dramatically with small assumption changes, we can't trust them. TV, Search, and Radio are robust‚Äîconclusions hold regardless of specification. Social and Display are sensitive‚Äîbe cautious making decisions based on these estimates.

The final step: translate technical results into actionable insights. Good reporting separates confident findings from uncertain ones, and acknowledges limitations honestly.

Limitations & Caveats

  • Model estimates average effects‚Äîindividual campaigns may vary
  • Assumes historical relationships continue (no major market changes)
  • Long-term brand effects may not be fully captured
  • Display may have unmeasured brand effects not reflected in short-term sales
  • Conducted sensitivity analysis across 6 specifications
  • All computational diagnostics passed

Why honest uncertainty is valuable

"TV ROI is probably 1.8-2.5" is more useful than "TV ROI is 2.1" because it's honest about what we actually know. You can confidently invest in TV knowing it's profitable across the entire range‚Äîwithout false precision that could mislead planning.

Similarly, acknowledging uncertainty about Social prevents over-investment in a channel that might not be working‚Äîwhile also preventing premature abandonment of something that might be.
