§ 01 · The problem

We're paying twice for measurement and getting half the answer

A confident-looking model is not the same as a correct model. Two paradigms are out of sync.

The marketing mix model (MMM) is the system of record for "what is each channel worth." It produces a clean ROI number per channel every quarter, and the planning team allocates next quarter's budget against those numbers. The model has a tight credible interval; the deck shows neat bar charts; the room agrees on the top three channels. Decisions get made.

Separately, we run geo-lift experiments — paid search holdouts, CTV market tests, audio matched-market trials — and each one produces its own causal estimate. Those estimates are also reported. They sometimes agree with the MMM and sometimes don't. When they disagree, one of two things happens: someone picks the source they trust more for that channel and moves on, or the disagreement gets escalated and unresolved.

Schematic · Two systems, no wiring
Today: MMM and experiments produce parallel answers that don't talk
Two well-built systems answering the same question, with no formal mechanism to combine them. The cost shows up as decisions made on the source the analyst happened to trust most that week.

The structural problem is not that either system is bad — both are well-built. The problem is that neither one is wired into the other. The MMM doesn't know which channels need experimental backup; the experiments don't feed back into the next MMM fit. So we keep paying for both, and we keep accepting whichever number the analyst chose to report.

▸ What this looks like in practice

Last cycle, the MMM said connected TV was a 1.4× ROI channel with a tight ±0.2 interval. A geo-lift test on the same channel came back at 2.1× with a wider but real interval. We had two answers, no formal way to combine them, and a budget meeting in two weeks. The decision was made on a hunch about which source was "more trustworthy in this case." That happens routinely. The cost is invisible because we never see what we would have done if the two had been combined properly.

Why a tight number isn't a true number

An MMM produces tight intervals when the model is well-regularized and the historical data tells a consistent story. But "consistent" is not the same as "correct" — when channels move together (national TV and digital flighted in unison; promo periods that overlap with seasonality), the model can't tell them apart, and a regularizing prior will produce a tight number anyway, anchored to an arbitrary point.

Experiments are the only tool that can break this tie. A randomized geo-lift on a single channel causes the spend variation, so the resulting ROI estimate isn't entangled with the rest of the marketing plan. That's the calibration leverage we are not currently extracting.

▸ For the technical audience

The argument here is the standard one: observational identification is conditional on the assumed DAG, while experimental identification is by design. The MMM posterior is a coherent belief under its assumptions; the geo-lift posterior is an unbiased likelihood under randomization. Combining them is conjugate Bayes — the math is in the Foundations tab. The piece this brief argues for is the operational coupling, not the math.

§ 02 · What we propose

Make the MMM and the experiments one loop

A single proposal in three words: prioritize, measure, calibrate. Then repeat.

The proposal is a quarterly cycle that ties the two systems together with no new measurement spend — only better routing of the spend already committed.

1

Read the MMM with epistemic discipline

For each channel, the MMM gives a point estimate and an uncertainty range. We currently use the point estimate. The proposal is to use both — the uncertainty range tells us where the model is least sure of itself.

2

Score channels for testability and stakes

Two questions per channel: How much would we learn from a clean experiment? (call this the learning score) and How much would the answer change our budget decision? (call this the value score). The first is high when the MMM is uncertain and the experiment can be precise; the second is high when the channel takes a meaningful share of spend and the answer might cross a decision threshold.

3

Run the top 2–3 experiments

The same experiment cadence we run today, but with the channel selection driven by the priority scores rather than by who-asked-first. Submodularity (a property of the math) means after about three experiments per cycle, additional ones add diminishing returns — so the cycle naturally caps the test program at a sensible level.

4

Feed the experimental result back into the next MMM fit

This is the new step. When the next MMM is fit, the channels that got tested have an "informed prior" — the model is told, in advance, that this channel's ROI has been measured experimentally and where it landed. The MMM then has to reconcile its observational evidence with the experimental evidence, producing a calibrated answer that draws from both.

5

Allocate budget from the calibrated model

Same allocation process as today, run against the calibrated MMM. The headline change is that the channels with the highest stakes now have causal backing, not just observational backing. The remaining channels have honestly-quantified uncertainty rather than a misleadingly-tight number.

6

Repeat

Each cycle, calibrated channels become "well-known"; their priority scores drop. Channels that used to be lower priority rise. Over four to six cycles, the program covers the channels that matter and the ones that move, with documented calibration shelf-life so we know when to refresh.

▸ The single-sentence summary

The MMM tells us where to look; the experiments tell us what's actually there; the next MMM bakes in what we learned. Each loop tightens the parts of the picture that matter most for budget decisions.

Where the dollars come from

The misallocation reduction is not a forecasted revenue lift — it is the gap between what the current allocation produces and what the allocation would produce if every channel were tuned to its calibrated ROI. That gap is closeable; closing it is the headline deliverable.

  • Reallocation lift. When we discover a channel is underperforming the MMM estimate, we move budget to channels with calibrated higher returns. This is direct revenue.
  • Confidence-tier transparency. Some channels' MMM numbers are tight because they are well-identified; others are tight because they are over-regularized. The loop separates these — and the second category is where misallocation hides.
  • Experimentation cost discipline. Submodularity means after the top 2–3 experiments per cycle, more testing is wasted spend. The framework caps the program at a sensible level without manual triage.
  • Audit trail. Every quarter we can answer "why did this channel's recommendation change?" with a calibrated posterior, not an analyst's adjustment notes.
§ 05 · Risks & mitigations

What could go wrong

Three honest failure modes and what we do about each.

Risk 1 · Experiment and MMM disagree publicly

Geo-lift comes back at 2.1×; the MMM said 1.4×. Without a calibration framework, this becomes a credibility problem. Mitigation: the framework explicitly handles this — the calibrated posterior is a precision-weighted compromise of both, and the disagreement itself becomes documented evidence of MMM specification fragility, which is information we want.

Risk 2 · The team takes longer than 90 days to ramp up

PyMC fluency and Bayesian framing are non-trivial. Mitigation: Phase 1 of the implementation plan runs the existing OLS pipeline in parallel with the new Bayesian fit. We don't decommission anything until two consecutive cycles produce comparable answers under audit.

Risk 3 · We over-rely on early experiments

A geo-lift run during an unusual season (peak holiday, supply shock, competitor launch) might calibrate the MMM to a non-representative regime. Mitigation: the framework includes information-decay modeling — calibrations have a documented shelf-life, and re-experimentation triggers fire when the calibration ages out.

Risk 4 · Stakeholders see "wider intervals" as a regression

The honest Bayesian MMM may produce wider credible intervals than the over-regularized OLS version, even when it's the more accurate answer. Mitigation: reporting templates explicitly include the confidence tier per channel; "wider but calibrated" is presented as the upgrade it is rather than a regression.

▸ The non-mitigation

The thing this proposal does not solve is bad data quality. Geo panel attribution gaps, channel definition drift, missing impressions data — none of these get better because we adopt a Bayesian framework. They need to be solved separately, and the implementation plan flags the data-quality prerequisites in Phase 1. We are not papering over data debt; we are sequencing the work.

§ 06 · The ask

What we need to start

A 90-day pilot, not a re-architecture. The decommission decision comes later.

The proposal is a 90-day Phase 1 pilot that delivers three things:

  1. A working Bayesian MMM running in parallel with the existing OLS pipeline. Same data, same outcomes, audited side-by-side. (Weeks 1–6.)
  2. The first priority map for the next quarter's experiment plan. This is the visible artifact a CFO or VP can sign off on. (Weeks 6–8.)
  3. The first calibration cycle: run the top 2–3 experiments selected by the priority map, then refit the Bayesian MMM with those results as informed priors. Compare allocation recommendations pre- and post-calibration. (Weeks 9–13.)

The Phase 1 deliverable is decision-grade evidence about whether to continue. If the priority map is recognizable, the calibrated allocation is sensible, and the predictive accuracy improves out-of-sample, we proceed to Phase 2 (steady-state quarterly cadence). If any of those checks fail, we have learned something specific about our data and can iterate or stop.

One Bayesian-fluent data scientist at ~50% allocation for the 90-day window, plus the existing measurement team's normal cycle time. No new vendor contracts, no incremental tooling spend (PyMC and friends are open-source). § 01 · Motivation

Why MMM posteriors are not enough

A tight credible interval is not the same as a correct estimate.

Marketing mix models combine months or years of observational data into a joint posterior over channel-level effects — saturation curves, adstock decay, contribution coefficients. Those posteriors are perfectly coherent given the model's assumptions and the variation present in the data. But two failure modes are routine:

  1. Underidentification masquerading as precision. When channels move together (e.g., national TV and digital programmatic flighted in unison), the data cannot tell them apart. A regularizing prior will produce a tight posterior anyway — but it is anchored to an arbitrary point along the unidentified ridge.
  2. Specification fragility. A single MMM is one specification among many plausible ones. Different choices of trend, seasonality, control variables, and adstock structure can produce ROI rankings that disagree by factors of 2× or more, even when each model individually has tight intervals.
▸ The core problem

An MMM posterior with a tight credible interval does not mean the model is well-identified. It may mean the model is confidently wrong. Experiments — which produce causal identification by construction — are the only device that can distinguish precision from accuracy.

Two complementary objectives

Once you accept that experiments are the calibration tool, you still face the question of which experiments. Two distinct objectives emerge, and a good framework integrates both:

Epistemic — reduce uncertainty

  • Quantify posterior entropy per channel
  • Pick experiments that maximally collapse the posterior
  • Prioritize where beliefs are least grounded
  • Metric: Expected Information Gain (EIG)

Instrumental — improve decisions

  • Map ROI uncertainty to budget decision quality
  • Prioritize where errors are most costly
  • Quantify the dollar value of resolving each uncertainty
  • Metric: Expected Value of Information (EVOI)

These two are correlated but not identical. A boutique channel may have huge posterior variance (high epistemic priority) but receive 1% of spend (low decision stakes). A workhorse channel may have moderate variance but receive 30% of spend, making any uncertainty extraordinarily costly. The full framework integrates both lenses — see the Information & Value tab for the formal treatment.

§ 02 · A Bayesian primer

Prior, likelihood, posterior — visualized

For readers without a Bayesian background — everything that follows hangs on this picture.

Bayesian inference is the discipline of updating beliefs in the face of new evidence. Three objects appear repeatedly in this guide:

  • Prior $p(\theta)$ — what you believe about an unknown parameter $\theta$ before seeing experimental data. For us, this is what the MMM tells us about a channel's ROI.
  • Likelihood $p(\hat{y} \mid \theta)$ — the probability of observing the experimental outcome $\hat{y}$ if the truth were $\theta$.
  • Posterior $p(\theta \mid \hat{y})$ — the updated belief about $\theta$ after seeing $\hat{y}$.

Bayes' rule glues them together:

$$p(\theta \mid \hat{y}) \;=\; \frac{p(\hat{y} \mid \theta)\, p(\theta)}{p(\hat{y})} \;\propto\; \underbrace{p(\hat{y} \mid \theta)}_{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \underbrace{p(\theta)}_{\text{prior}}$$ Eq. 1 · Bayes' rule

For the rest of this guide, $\theta$ will denote a channel-level causal parameter (typically ROI or elasticity), and $\hat{y}$ will denote the noisy estimate produced by an experiment. The MMM provides $p(\theta)$. The experiment provides $p(\hat{y} \mid \theta)$. The product is what we want.

▸ For the commercial reader

Forget the symbols for a moment. The structure here is just: start with a belief, see new evidence, update the belief. The clever part is that "how much you update" depends on how confident the prior was and how precise the new evidence is. A confident prior pushes back against weak evidence; a precise experiment overwhelms a vague prior. The interactive chart below lets you drag those four numbers and watch the answer shift.

The Gaussian conjugate update

When the prior is Gaussian and the experiment likelihood is Gaussian (the standard assumption for geo-lift difference-in-differences), the math simplifies dramatically. With prior $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$ and likelihood $\hat{y} \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma_e^2)$, the posterior is also Gaussian:

$$\theta \mid \hat{y} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu_{\text{post}},\; \sigma_{\text{post}}^2\right)$$ $$\sigma_{\text{post}}^{-2} \;=\; \sigma_0^{-2} + \sigma_e^{-2} \qquad \mu_{\text{post}} \;=\; \sigma_{\text{post}}^2\!\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{\hat{y}}{\sigma_e^2}\right)$$ Eq. 2 · Gaussian conjugate update
Plain English

Precision (the inverse of variance) is additive. The posterior precision is the prior precision plus the experiment's precision. The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the experimental estimate — whichever is more precise pulls the posterior more strongly toward itself.

This single fact is the workhorse for almost everything that follows. Both EIG (in the Information & Value tab) and the calibration mechanism (in The Loop tab) are direct consequences of Equation 2.

§ 03 · Formal problem setup

The decision structure

State space, action space, observation model, utility — the four ingredients of any decision problem.

State space

Let $\theta_k$ denote the true causal ROI (or elasticity) for channel $k \in \{1,\dots,K\}$. The MMM produces a joint posterior over the full vector $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \dots, \theta_K)$:

$$p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; p\!\left(\theta_1, \theta_2, \dots, \theta_K \;\big|\; y, X, \mathcal{M}\right)$$ $$p(\theta_k) \;=\; \int p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\, d\boldsymbol{\theta}_{-k}$$ Eq. 3 · MMM posterior & marginal

Where $y$ is the historical outcome series, $X$ is the design matrix of media spend and controls, and $\mathcal{M}$ is the choice of model (specification). Throughout this guide we approximate the marginal as $\theta_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)$ — accurate when posteriors are well-behaved and good enough for prioritization even when they are not.

Action space

The planner selects an experimental portfolio $A \subseteq \{1, \dots, K\}$ together with a design $d_k$ for each chosen channel:

$$A \;=\; \{(k_1, d_1),\, (k_2, d_2),\, \dots\}$$ $$d \;=\; \{n_{\text{test}},\, n_{\text{ctrl}},\, \Delta_{\text{spend}},\, \text{duration},\, \text{assignment}\}$$ $$\text{subject to}\quad |A| \le C_{\text{ops}},\quad \text{cost}(A) \le B$$ Eq. 4 · Portfolio & design

Observation model

Each experiment produces a noisy estimate of the true causal effect. For a geo-lift difference-in-differences (DiD) design, this is approximately Gaussian:

$$\hat{\tau}_k \mid \tau_k, d \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\tau_k,\; \sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2(d)\right)$$ Eq. 5 · Experimental likelihood

The experimental noise variance $\sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2$ is a deterministic function of the design choices — number of geos, baseline geo-level variance, spend delta size, and pre-period length. A simple but useful approximation:

$$\sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2(d) \;\approx\; \frac{2\, s^2_{\text{geo},k}}{n\, T} \cdot \frac{1}{\Delta^2_{\text{spend},k}}$$ Eq. 6 · DiD design noise (approx.)

where $s^2_{\text{geo},k}$ is the residual geo-week variance from the pre-period, $n$ is geos per arm, and $T$ is the test duration in weeks. This formula is approximate but captures the right scaling: more geos, longer tests, and bigger spend deltas all shrink experimental noise.

The planning horizon

The framework operates over a planning cycle: current MMM → portfolio decision → experiments run → posterior update → next MMM refit. Decisions made now affect beliefs available for the next budget cycle, which introduces a natural sequential / adaptive structure (covered in The Loop tab).

▸ Notation we will use throughout

$\theta_k$ — true channel-$k$ parameter (ROI). $\mu_k, \sigma_k^2$ — MMM posterior mean and variance. $\tau_k$ — same parameter as $\theta_k$ but viewed through the experimental lens. $\hat{y}_k$ or $\hat{\tau}_k$ — the experimental estimate. $\sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2$ — experimental noise variance. $b_k$ — budget allocated to channel $k$. $s_k$ — channel-$k$ spend share.

§ 01 · Epistemic criterion

Expected information gain

Maximize the expected reduction in posterior entropy over channel parameters.

Schematic · What EIG measures
A high-EIG experiment shrinks the posterior; a low-EIG one barely moves it
Prior — current MMM uncertainty Expected posterior — what we'd believe afterward ΔH bar — entropy reduction in bits
EIG is the entropy reduction from prior to expected posterior, measured in bits (right gauge). Two ingredients matter: how loose the current belief is (σ₀) and how precise the experiment will be (σ_e). Same total mass under each curve; only the width changes — when the posterior collapses, ΔH is large.

Definition & intuition

For a given channel $k$ and design $d$, the Expected Information Gain is the expected KL divergence from the prior (current MMM posterior) to the updated posterior, integrated over what experimental outcome we might see:

$$\mathrm{EIG}(k, d) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\hat{y}_k}\Big[ \mathrm{KL}\big(\, p(\theta_k \mid \hat{y}_k, d) \,\big\|\, p(\theta_k) \,\big) \Big]$$ Eq. 7 · EIG via KL divergence

This has an equivalent and often more tractable form: it is the mutual information between the unknown parameter and the experimental outcome:

$$\mathrm{EIG}(k, d) \;=\; I(\theta_k\,;\,\hat{y}_k \mid d) \;=\; \underbrace{H[p(\theta_k)]}_{\text{prior entropy}} \;-\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{\hat{y}_k}\!\big[H[p(\theta_k \mid \hat{y}_k)]\big]}_{\text{expected posterior entropy}}$$ Eq. 8 · EIG as mutual information
Plain English

EIG asks: before running the experiment, by how much do we expect our uncertainty about this channel to shrink, on average, across all the outcomes we might see? It's measured in bits (or nats). One bit means uncertainty halves. Three bits means it shrinks 8-fold.

▸ Why a stakeholder cares about EIG

EIG is the "learning score" from the Story tab. It answers a single question for each candidate experiment: before we spend money running this, how much would we expect to know afterward that we don't know now? Channels where the MMM is already confident don't have much to teach us; channels where the MMM is unsure but the experiment can be precise have a lot to teach us. EIG is just that comparison made formal.

The Gaussian closed form — derived

Under the Gaussian-Gaussian setup of the Foundations tab, EIG has a remarkably clean closed form. Here is the full derivation, because every step generalizes a useful idea.

Step 1. The differential entropy of a Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ is

$$H[\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)] \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\big(2\pi e\, \sigma^2\big)$$ Eq. 9

Step 2. The conjugate update tells us the posterior variance — and crucially, this variance is not random. It depends only on $\sigma_0^2$ and $\sigma_e^2$, not on the realized value of $\hat{y}$:

$$\sigma_{\text{post}}^2 \;=\; \left( \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{1}{\sigma_e^2} \right)^{-1} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_0^2 \sigma_e^2}{\sigma_0^2 + \sigma_e^2}$$ Eq. 10

Step 3. Because $\sigma_{\text{post}}^2$ is constant, the expected posterior entropy is just $\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\,\sigma_{\text{post}}^2)$. Substituting into Eq. 8 and cancelling the $2\pi e$ terms:

$$\mathrm{EIG}(k, d) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\left( \frac{\sigma_k^2}{\sigma_{\text{post},k}^2} \right) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\left( 1 + \frac{\sigma_k^2}{\sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2(d)} \right)$$ Eq. 11 · Gaussian EIG closed form ★
▸ Read this formula carefully

Equation 11 is the single most useful formula in this guide. It tells you that information gain depends only on a signal-to-noise ratio: the channel's prior variance divided by the experiment's noise variance. Doubling either has the same effect. A noisy experiment on an uncertain channel can yield as much information as a precise experiment on a moderately uncertain one.

Monte Carlo EIG for non-Gaussian posteriors

Real MMM posteriors — especially those with non-conjugate priors, hierarchical structure, or skewed contributions — are rarely exactly Gaussian. For these cases, a nested Monte Carlo estimator works:

# Nested Monte Carlo EIG estimator (PyMC-style pseudocode)
def eig_mc(prior_samples, simulate_exp, log_lik, d, N=2000):
    """
    prior_samples: array of θ draws from MMM posterior (acts as prior here)
    simulate_exp:  function θ -> simulated experimental outcome ŷ
    log_lik:       function (ŷ, θ) -> log p(ŷ | θ, d)
    """
    eig = 0.0
    for i in range(N):
        theta_i = prior_samples[i]
        y_i     = simulate_exp(theta_i, d)
        # inner: log marginal likelihood, p(ŷ_i)
        log_marg = logsumexp([log_lik(y_i, prior_samples[j]) for j in range(N)]) - log(N)
        # outer: log conditional likelihood, p(ŷ_i | θ_i)
        log_cond = log_lik(y_i, theta_i)
        eig += (log_cond - log_marg)
    return eig / N

This is $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ in samples; for production use, the VNMC (variational nested Monte Carlo) bound or amortized neural estimators (e.g., InfoNCE) bring this down to $\mathcal{O}(N)$. For prioritization purposes, the Gaussian approximation in Eq. 11 is almost always sufficient — Monte Carlo refinement is reserved for borderline channels.

§ 02 · Instrumental criterion

Expected value of information

Prioritize experiments that most improve the quality of budget allocation decisions.

Why EIG alone is not enough

EIG treats every bit of uncertainty reduction as equally valuable. But uncertainty about a channel receiving 1% of spend is far less costly than equivalent uncertainty about a channel receiving 40% of spend. The Expected Value of Information framework prices uncertainty in dollars, accounting for the asymmetric cost of being wrong.

The decision-quality framing

Define the downstream action as a budget allocation $\mathbf{b} = (b_1, \dots, b_K)$ with $\sum_k b_k = B$. The organization picks $\mathbf{b}^*$ based on its current beliefs and earns utility according to true parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$U(\mathbf{b}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} \theta_k \cdot f(b_k)$$ Eq. 12 · Utility as response sum

where $f(\cdot)$ is a (usually concave / saturating) channel response function. The expected utility under current beliefs (no experiment) is:

$$\mathrm{EU}_{\text{now}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta} \sim p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}\!\Big[ U\!\left(\mathbf{b}^*(\bar{\boldsymbol{\theta}}),\, \boldsymbol{\theta}\right) \Big] \quad\text{where}\quad \mathbf{b}^*(\bar{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) \;=\; \arg\max_{\mathbf{b}}\, U(\mathbf{b}, \bar{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$$ Eq. 13 · Expected utility, no experiment

If we instead run experiment $k$ first, we will see $\hat{y}_k$, update our beliefs, then reoptimize the allocation. The expected utility of that strategy is:

$$\mathrm{EU}_{\text{after},k} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\hat{y}_k}\!\Big[ \max_{\mathbf{b}}\, \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \hat{y}_k}\!\big[U(\mathbf{b}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\big] \Big]$$ Eq. 14 · Expected utility, after experiment $k$

EVOI & EVPI

The Expected Value of Information for experimenting on channel $k$ is the difference:

$$\mathrm{EVOI}(k) \;=\; \mathrm{EU}_{\text{after},k} \;-\; \mathrm{EU}_{\text{now}}$$ Eq. 15 · EVOI ★

An upper bound is the Expected Value of Perfect Information — the value of learning $\theta_k$ exactly, with no experimental noise:

$$\mathrm{EVPI}(k) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\theta_k}\!\Big[ \max_{\mathbf{b}}\, U(\mathbf{b}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) \Big] \;-\; \mathrm{EU}_{\text{now}}$$ $$\mathrm{EVOI}(k) \;\le\; \mathrm{EVPI}(k)$$ Eq. 16 · EVPI bound

The ratio $\mathrm{EVOI}(k)\,/\,\mathrm{EVPI}(k)$ is a useful efficiency metric — it tells you what fraction of the maximum possible value your design captures.

The linear case & the sign-flip intuition

Closed-form EVOI is generally hard, but a useful approximation drops out of a linearized utility model. Suppose around the current allocation, the marginal utility of channel-$k$ spend is approximately $\theta_k \cdot \partial f / \partial b_k$. A budget reallocation only happens if the experiment changes the ordering of channels — i.e., if the posterior mean crosses a decision threshold. Then:

$$\mathrm{EVOI}(k) \;\approx\; B \cdot s_k \cdot \mathbb{E}\!\left[\,\big|\hat{\theta}_{k,\text{post}} - \hat{\theta}_{k,\text{prior}}\big|\,\right] \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{decision flips})$$ Eq. 17 · Linear EVOI approximation
Plain English

EVOI is large when (i) the budget at stake is big, (ii) the channel takes a lot of that budget, (iii) we expect the experimental result to substantially shift the posterior mean, and (iv) there's a real chance the shift will cross a decision threshold (e.g., flip from "underspend" to "overspend").

Each line shows EVOI vs. channel spend share, holding posterior uncertainty fixed. Lines correspond to different "distance from decision threshold" — channels whose posterior mean already sits comfortably on one side of the decision boundary have lower EVOI than channels straddling it.

Structural drivers of EVOI

  • High prior uncertainty ($\sigma_k$ large) — wide credible intervals mean large potential for belief revision, increasing EVOI.
  • High spend share ($s_k$ large) — channels receiving big budget fractions have higher stakes; errors cost more.
  • Decision threshold proximity — when the posterior mean is near a budget reallocation threshold (e.g., borderline ROI = 1.0), even small uncertainty reduction has high value.
  • Convex response (saturation) — when the response function is concave, correcting misallocation has compounding returns.
  • Near-optimal sensitivity — if the current allocation is near the unconstrained optimum for a channel, even large ROI errors can have low EVOI (the decision wouldn't change much).
▸ Practical caveat

EVOI requires an explicit downstream decision model — a specification of how ROI estimates translate into budget changes. This is often left implicit in practice. Make it explicit. A simple rule: reallocate proportional to posterior mean ROI, subject to floor/ceiling constraints. A richer model: optimizer with saturation curves and channel-level minimums. The EVOI computation is only as good as the assumed decision rule.

§ 03 · Prioritization

Combining EIG & EVOI into a priority score

Jointly optimize for what we learn and how much it improves our decisions.

The composite score

Define a per-channel priority score as a weighted combination of normalized EIG and EVOI, adjusted for experiment cost and operational risk:

$$\mathrm{Score}(k) \;=\; \lambda \cdot \widetilde{\mathrm{EIG}}(k, d^*) \;+\; (1-\lambda) \cdot \widetilde{\mathrm{EVOI}}(k) \;-\; \gamma \cdot \mathrm{cost}(k, d^*) \;-\; \rho \cdot \mathrm{risk}(k)$$ Eq. 18 · Composite priority score

where $\widetilde{\cdot}$ denotes min-max normalization to $[0, 1]$ across channels, $\lambda \in [0, 1]$ trades off epistemic vs. instrumental value, $\gamma$ penalizes expensive experiments, $\rho$ penalizes operational risk (geo conflicts, brand sensitivity), and $d^*$ is the best feasible design for channel $k$.

Submodularity & greedy selection

A non-obvious but enormously useful fact: the EIG of a set of experiments is a submodular function of the set. Submodularity means diminishing returns — adding the same experiment to a small portfolio yields more information than adding it to a large one. Formally, for sets $A \subseteq A'$:

$$\mathrm{EIG}(A \cup \{k\}) - \mathrm{EIG}(A) \;\ge\; \mathrm{EIG}(A' \cup \{k\}) - \mathrm{EIG}(A')$$ Eq. 19 · Submodularity of EIG

Submodularity has a major practical payoff: greedy selection is provably near-optimal. The classical result (Nemhauser–Wolsey–Fisher, 1978) states that for any submodular monotone function with a cardinality constraint, the greedy algorithm achieves at least $(1 - 1/e) \approx 63\%$ of the optimum:

# Greedy portfolio construction
A = []
while len(A) < C_ops:
    feasible = [k for k in channels if is_feasible(k, A)]
    if not feasible: break
    k_star = argmax(feasible, key=lambda k: score(k, A))
    A.append(k_star)
# By submodularity: EIG(A_greedy) ≥ (1 - 1/e) · EIG(A_optimal)

§ 01 · The calibration loop

Using experiments to calibrate future MMMs

Geo-lift results are informative likelihoods. Use them as structured constraints on the next MMM.

What calibration means

Calibration is the process of using experimental causal estimates to anchor the MMM's channel parameters so that model-implied ROIs are consistent with ground truth. This can range from soft regularization (Bayesian priors informed by experiments) to hard constraints (fixing parameters at experimental point estimates). Three mechanisms cover the main use cases.

Before calibration

  • MMM parameters identified by observational variation alone
  • Posteriors driven mostly by structural assumptions
  • Attribution may reflect media collinearity, not causality
  • ROI ranking unstable across model specifications

After calibration

  • Experimental likelihoods constrain key channel parameters
  • Posteriors tighter on tested channels; partial pooling propagates
  • Attribution anchored to causal reality for tested channels
  • Remaining uncertainty quantified, not hidden

Mechanism 1 · Soft calibration via Bayesian priors

Schematic · Three calibration mechanisms
How experimental evidence enters the next MMM
M1 reshapes the prior on θₖ for next quarter's fit. M2 enters the experimental observation alongside MMM data in one joint likelihood. M3 propagates evidence from a tested channel to untested siblings via a shared category-level hyperparameter. Choose by overlap between experiment window and MMM training window — and by how many channels you can test.

The most principled approach uses the experimental posterior as the prior for the next MMM fit. The experiment on channel $k$ produces $p(\tau_k \mid \hat{y}_k)$ — pass that distribution as the prior on the corresponding MMM parameter:

$$p(\tau_k \mid \hat{y}_k) \;\propto\; p(\hat{y}_k \mid \tau_k) \cdot p(\tau_k)$$ $$p(\theta_k) \;\leftarrow\; p(\tau_k \mid \hat{y}_k) \quad \text{(with appropriate scale transform)}$$ $$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid y, X) \;\propto\; p(y \mid X, \boldsymbol{\theta}) \cdot p(\theta_k \mid \hat{y}_k) \cdot p(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{-k})$$ Eq. 20 · Soft calibration via prior

The likelihood pulls unconstrained channels in fitting; the informative prior anchors the calibrated channel near its experimentally-identified value. The scale transform matters: a geo-lift τ̂ is in outcome units per spend-dollar in the test window, while an MMM coefficient may be in standardized units. Convert carefully.

Mechanism 2 · Likelihood augmentation

An alternative: treat the experimental estimate as an additional data point in the MMM likelihood. This is cleaner when the experiment overlaps the MMM training window:

$$\log p(y, \hat{y}_k \mid X, \boldsymbol{\theta}) \;=\; \log p(y \mid X, \boldsymbol{\theta}) \;+\; \log p(\hat{y}_k \mid \theta_k)$$ $$p(\hat{y}_k \mid \theta_k) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(g(\theta_k),\; \sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2\right)$$ Eq. 21 · Augmented likelihood

where $g(\theta_k)$ maps MMM channel parameters to the implied geo-lift effect size — accounting for the spend delta in the test, the saturation curve evaluated at the test spend level, and any adstock effects in the experiment window. Constructing $g(\cdot)$ correctly is the hardest part of this mechanism; miscalibration introduces systematic bias.

Mechanism 3 · Hierarchical pooling

When experiments cover only some channels, hierarchical pooling propagates information to untested channels via shared structural parameters:

$$\theta_k \mid \mu_{\text{cat}}, \sigma_{\text{cat}} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu_{\text{cat}[k]},\; \sigma_{\text{cat}[k]}^2\right)$$ Eq. 22 · Category-level partial pooling

where $\mathrm{cat}[k]$ is the media category of channel $k$ (e.g., video, search, social). An experiment on CTV updates $\mu_{\text{video}}$, which partially informs the OLV estimate. Pooling strength is controlled by $\sigma_{\text{cat}}$ via a hyperprior — tighter $\sigma_{\text{cat}}$ pools more strongly.

Calibration validity checks

  • Scale consistency — verify that the experimental $\hat{\tau}_k$ and the MMM-implied effect use the same outcome variable and time aggregation.
  • Coverage check — confirm that the MMM prior 95% CI for channel $k$ covers the experimental point estimate. If not, diagnose model misspecification before calibrating.
  • Quality gate — only use experiments that pass pre-specified quality checks (balance test, pre-period MAPE, sufficient MDE, no contamination evidence) for calibration inputs.
  • Attribution reconciliation — after refitting, confirm that total attributed conversions still match observed outcomes — calibration should not break aggregate coherence.
  • Predictive validation — hold out a test period and compare pre- vs. post-calibration MAPE. Calibration should improve predictive accuracy or at minimum not degrade it.
▸ When experiment and MMM disagree

If the experimental estimate falls outside the MMM posterior's plausible range, do not automatically trust either side. Diagnose: (1) Did the experiment have sufficient power? (2) Was there contamination or geo spillover? (3) Is the MMM response curve evaluated at the correct spend level? (4) Are there temporal confounders in the experiment window? Work through this checklist before resolving the conflict.

T₂

Make budget decisions from the calibrated MMM. Document which channels are experiment-backed vs. model-only. Report confidence tiering to stakeholders — distinguish evidence quality from point estimates.

T₅

Calibrated channels now have tighter posteriors — their EIG and EVOI drop. Recompute the priority grid with updated beliefs. Previously deprioritized channels may now rise. Begin the next cycle.

Information decay & re-experimentation triggers

Experimental calibration has a shelf life. An experiment from 18 months ago, run under different competitive conditions, spend levels, or creative strategies, may no longer accurately represent current channel effectiveness. Model an exponential decay of experimental information over time:

$$\sigma_{k,\text{eff}}^2(t) \;=\; \sigma_{k,\text{post}}^2 \cdot \exp(\lambda_{\text{decay}} \cdot t)$$ Eq. 23 · Information decay

where $\lambda_{\text{decay}}$ is calibrated from observed year-over-year MMM coefficient stability. As $\sigma_{k,\text{eff}}^2$ grows, EIG and EVOI for re-experimentation rise again, creating a principled re-experimentation schedule.

Adaptive stopping criteria

A channel exits the active experimentation pool when all three conditions hold:

$$\mathrm{EIG}(k, d^*) \;<\; \varepsilon_{\text{EIG}} \quad\text{AND}\quad \mathrm{EVOI}(k) \;<\; \varepsilon_{\text{EVOI}} \quad\text{AND}\quad \mathrm{age}(\text{last\_exp}_k) \;<\; T_{\text{refresh}}$$ Eq. 24 · Stopping conditions

$T_{\text{refresh}}$ depends on media market dynamics, creative/targeting shifts, and competitive changes. Typical values: 12–24 months for stable channels, 6–12 months for fast-moving digital surfaces.

The point of running this loop is not any single experiment — it's the compounding of belief updates over successive cycles. A useful way to see the value is to plot the trajectories of four quantities that each cycle is trying to move:

  1. MMM posterior precision — the 95% credible interval width on each channel's ROI. As experiments calibrate channels, widths contract.
  2. Budget allocation — the share of total spend going to each channel. As ROIs become better-identified, allocations migrate toward the (unknown but increasingly well-estimated) optimum.
  3. Misallocation cost — the gap between current spend and the spend that would maximize portfolio outcome under the current posterior. This is the running tab of wasted dollars per week.
  4. Portfolio marginal ROI — the marginal return of the next dollar across the portfolio. Rises as misallocation falls.

▸ Closing principle

The MMM and geo-lift experiments are not competing measurement paradigms — they are complementary nodes in a Bayesian inference graph. The MMM provides a coherent joint model of all channels with full coverage; experiments provide local causal identification for high-priority channels. Over successive cycles, this adaptive loop systematically contracts the uncertainty that actually matters for budget decisions.

§ 01 · Frequentist view

Frequentist estimators in the loop

DiD, synthetic control, power, and MDE — and how they map onto the same framework.

Why this section exists

Most geo-lift estimators in active production use are frequentist by construction: difference-in-differences with two-way fixed effects, synthetic control, augmented synthetic control, and time-based regression are all designed under sampling-theory assumptions and reported in the language of confidence intervals, standard errors, and p-values. Stakeholders speak that language. Pre-registration documents demand it. Regulators sometimes require it.

None of this is in tension with the Bayesian framework above. A frequentist point estimate paired with a standard error is — under mild assumptions — a Gaussian likelihood. That likelihood plugs straight into Equation 2 alongside the MMM prior, and the calibration loop continues unchanged. This section makes that bridge concrete.

▸ The mental model

Run frequentist estimators because they are well-understood, defensible, and pre-registrable. Interpret their outputs as Gaussian likelihoods feeding into the Bayesian update from The Loop tab. Stakeholders see CIs and p-values; the MMM team sees a precision-weighted posterior. Both are correct views of the same numbers.

Four estimators worth knowing

Geo-lift measurement converges on a small set of estimators. Below is a working reference — enough to understand the design choices, and enough to know what each one's standard error actually reflects.

1 · Difference-in-Differences (DiD) with two-way fixed effects

The standard panel-data workhorse. Stack treated and control geos over pre and post periods; identify the treatment effect from the differential change.

$$Y_{it} \;=\; \alpha_i \;+\; \lambda_t \;+\; \beta\, D_{it} \;+\; \mathbf{x}_{it}^{\top}\boldsymbol{\gamma} \;+\; \varepsilon_{it}$$ Eq. 25 · TWFE specification

where $\alpha_i$ are geo fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are time fixed effects, $D_{it} \in \{0,1\}$ is the treatment indicator, and $\mathbf{x}_{it}$ are time-varying controls (e.g. seasonality, weather, holidays). The estimand $\beta$ is the average treatment effect on the treated under parallel-trends.

Standard errors must be cluster-robust at the geo level. Default OLS standard errors will be badly anti-conservative because residuals are correlated within geo across time. Use the cluster-robust ("CR1") variance estimator, or for very small numbers of clusters, the wild cluster bootstrap. With $G$ geos, $T$ periods per geo, and within-geo serial correlation $\rho$, the inflation factor over independence is approximately:

$$\frac{\mathrm{Var}_{\text{cluster}}(\hat{\beta})}{\mathrm{Var}_{\text{iid}}(\hat{\beta})} \;\approx\; 1 \;+\; (T-1)\,\rho$$ Eq. 26 · Moulton-style inflation

For a typical geo-lift with $T = 16$ weekly observations and $\rho \approx 0.3$, this is roughly a 5× variance inflation — meaning naive standard errors understate uncertainty by a factor of ~$\sqrt{5} \approx 2.2$. Reporting the wrong SE is the most common preventable error in geo-lift readouts.

2 · Synthetic Control Method (SCM)

When few geos are treated and a single, well-matched counterfactual is needed, SCM constructs a weighted combination of donor geos that tracks the treated geo(s) in the pre-period:

$$\hat{Y}_{1t}^{\text{synth}} \;=\; \sum_{j \in \mathcal{D}} w_j\, Y_{jt}, \qquad \mathbf{w} \;=\; \arg\min_{\mathbf{w} \in \Delta} \sum_{t \in \text{pre}} \big(Y_{1t} \;-\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{Y}_{\mathcal{D},t}\big)^2$$ Eq. 27 · SCM weights (simplex-constrained)

where $\mathcal{D}$ is the donor pool and $\Delta$ is the simplex (weights non-negative, summing to 1). Inference is typically done via permutation: refit the procedure pretending each donor were treated, and compare the actual treatment effect to the placebo distribution.

3 · Augmented Synthetic Control (ASC)

SCM struggles when no convex combination of donors fits the treated geo well in the pre-period — extrapolation bias creeps in. ASC corrects for this by running an outcome model and using SCM only on the residuals:

$$\hat{\tau}_t^{\text{ASC}} \;=\; \underbrace{\big(Y_{1t} - \hat{Y}_{1t}^{\text{synth}}\big)}_{\text{SCM estimate}} \;-\; \underbrace{\big(\hat{m}(X_{1t}) - \sum_j w_j\,\hat{m}(X_{jt})\big)}_{\text{outcome-model bias correction}}$$ Eq. 28 · ASC point estimate

where $\hat{m}(\cdot)$ is a flexible regression model (ridge, gradient-boosted trees, neural net) trained on donor outcomes. ASC dominates plain SCM in poor pre-period fit regimes and reduces to SCM when the outcome model is degenerate. For inference, jackknife or conformal procedures replace simple permutation when the bias correction is non-trivial.

4 · Time-Based Regression (TBR) & Geo-Based Regression (GBR)

TBR fits a pre-period model relating treated-geo outcomes to control-geo outcomes, then forecasts the counterfactual through the test window. GBR is the cross-sectional analogue: regress per-geo outcome differences on treatment indicator with geo-level controls. Both are special cases of more general regression-based counterfactuals; both are cleanly summarized by a point estimate and a standard error suitable for the bridge equation below.

Power, MDE, and the connection to $\sigma_{\text{exp}}$

The frequentist design vocabulary centers on three quantities: power ($1 - \beta$, probability of detecting a true effect), significance level ($\alpha$, false-positive rate), and minimum detectable effect (MDE, smallest effect size detectable at the chosen power). For a two-sample mean comparison with per-arm sample size $n$ and outcome variance $s^2$, the standard sample-size formula is:

$$n \;=\; \frac{2\,(z_{\alpha/2} + z_{\beta})^2\, s^2}{\Delta^2} \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \mathrm{MDE} \;=\; (z_{\alpha/2} + z_{\beta})\,\sqrt{\frac{2 s^2}{n}}$$ Eq. 29 · Power / MDE relationship

For a panel design with $G$ geos per arm and $T$ post-period weeks, $s^2$ is replaced by the geo-week residual variance after partialling out fixed effects, and divided by $T$:

$$\sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2 \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{SE}^2(\hat{\beta}_k)}{1} \;\approx\; \frac{2\, s^2_{\text{geo},k}\,(1 + (T-1)\rho)}{G\, T \,\Delta_{\text{spend},k}^2}$$ Eq. 30 · σ_exp from a panel-data design

This is the same $\sigma_{\text{exp},k}^2$ used throughout the EIG/EVOI machinery in the Information & Value tab — it is just the squared standard error of the frequentist estimator. The frequentist designer minimizes MDE by choosing $G$ and $T$; the Bayesian designer minimizes $\sigma_{\text{exp},k}$ to maximize EIG. They are doing the same calculation in different notation.

The bridge equation

Given any frequentist estimator that returns a point estimate $\hat{\beta}_k$ and a standard error $\mathrm{SE}_k$, treat the result as a Gaussian likelihood:

$$p(\hat{\beta}_k \mid \theta_k) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\hat{\beta}_k;\, \theta_k,\, \mathrm{SE}_k^2\right)$$ Eq. 31 · Frequentist output as Gaussian likelihood

Combined with the MMM prior $\theta_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)$, the calibrated posterior is the standard conjugate update:

$$\sigma_{\text{cal},k}^{-2} \;=\; \sigma_k^{-2} + \mathrm{SE}_k^{-2} \qquad \mu_{\text{cal},k} \;=\; \sigma_{\text{cal},k}^2\!\left(\frac{\mu_k}{\sigma_k^2} + \frac{\hat{\beta}_k}{\mathrm{SE}_k^2}\right)$$ Eq. 32 · Bridge: frequentist → Bayesian update

This is mathematically identical to a precision-weighted average of the MMM posterior mean and the experimental point estimate. The frequentist team can run their analysis exactly as they would have anyway; the bridge only requires that they hand off $(\hat{\beta}_k, \mathrm{SE}_k)$ rather than just a verdict.

Caveats and edge cases

  • Non-Gaussian likelihoods — when $\hat{\beta}$ has a heavy-tailed sampling distribution (small clusters, weak instruments, very skewed outcomes), treating it as Gaussian via Eq. 31 is approximate. For permutation-based or jackknife-based inference, a kernel-smoothed empirical likelihood is more honest. The framework still works; the math is just numerical rather than closed-form.
  • Specification uncertainty — frequentist standard errors are conditional on the model specification being correct. Multiple-specification searching (different fixed effects, different control sets, different windows) inflates the realized error rate. Pre-register the specification, or use specification-curve analysis to bound the effect of model selection.
  • Cluster count — cluster-robust SEs require enough clusters (rule of thumb: 30+). With fewer geos, use wild cluster bootstrap or randomization inference. Reporting a TWFE coefficient with 8 treated geos and a textbook cluster-robust SE is one of the easier ways to get a misleading readout.
  • Heterogeneous treatment effects — when treatment timing varies across geos (staggered adoption), classic TWFE can yield negative weights on early-treated units, biasing the average effect. Use Callaway–Sant'Anna, de Chaisemartin–D'Haultfœuille, or the Borusyak–Jaravel–Spiess imputation estimator instead.
  • One-sided vs two-sided tests — the translation between p-values and posterior tail probabilities depends on the test direction. Be explicit about which is being reported, and flag any post-hoc switching from two-sided to one-sided framing.

When to use which paradigm

Use frequentist tools when…

  • The audience expects p-values, CIs, and pre-registration documents.
  • Regulatory or compliance constraints require sampling-theory framing.
  • The single experiment is the entire output — no MMM to combine with.
  • The team is more comfortable defending a TWFE specification than a prior.
  • Clean identification (parallel trends, donor matching) is the primary credibility argument.

Use Bayesian framing when…

  • You need to combine MMM beliefs with experimental evidence.
  • You are running the multi-cycle calibration loop.
  • You want EIG/EVOI for prioritization and adaptive design.
  • Direct probability statements ("70% chance ROI exceeds 1.5") are valuable to the decision-maker.
  • You need to propagate uncertainty through a downstream optimizer.
▸ The pragmatic recommendation

Run the experiment with the most defensible frequentist estimator for the design (TWFE with cluster-robust SE for clean DiD; ASC for poor pre-period fits; permutation inference for small donor pools). Report the frequentist verdict to stakeholders. Internally, hand off $(\hat{\beta}_k, \mathrm{SE}_k)$ to the calibration pipeline via Eq. 32, and let the next MMM refit absorb the new information. You get the rigor of pre-registered frequentist inference and the coherence of sequential Bayesian calibration — not one or the other.

§ 02 · Bayesian alternatives

Going fully Bayesian on the experiment side

Methods that go beyond the bridge — when the experiment itself is Bayesian.

When the bridge isn't enough

The bridge equation (Eq. 32) lets a frequentist estimator's output flow cleanly into the Bayesian calibration loop. That covers a lot of ground. But four situations argue for running the experiment-side analysis itself in a Bayesian framework rather than after-the-fact translation:

  1. Outlier-prone outcome data. Geo-week revenue and conversion series have heavy tails — promotions, stockouts, viral days. Gaussian likelihoods misrepresent uncertainty around extremes; Student-t likelihoods (the foundation of BEST) absorb them gracefully without ad-hoc trimming or winsorization.
  2. Single-treated-unit geo-lift. When only a handful of geos are treated, frequentist large-sample asymptotics are unreliable. Bayesian counterfactual forecasting (CausalImpact / BSTS) is purpose-built for the small-N regime and produces honest intervals on the cumulative effect.
  3. Heterogeneous geo-level effects. When treatment effects vary meaningfully across markets, hierarchical Bayesian models give principled partial pooling — automatic shrinkage of noisy per-geo estimates with full uncertainty propagation. Fixed-effects-only specifications cannot do this.
  4. Sequential or adaptive designs. Frequentist sequential testing requires explicit alpha-spending corrections that compound across data looks. Bayesian sequential inference doesn't, because posteriors are valid at any stopping time.

In each case, the resulting posterior plugs into the same calibration loop — but now without the Gaussian-likelihood approximation in Eq. 31, and with strictly more information available downstream.

BEST: robust Bayesian estimation

Kruschke's Bayesian Estimation Supersedes the t-Test (BEST, 2013) replaces the two-sample t-test with full posterior estimation under a Student-t likelihood. The minimal specification for a treated/control comparison:

$$y_g \;\sim\; t_\nu(\mu_g,\, \sigma_g), \qquad g \in \{\text{treat},\, \text{control}\}$$ Eq. 33 · BEST likelihood
$$\mu_g \sim \mathcal{N}(m_0,\, s_0^2), \qquad \sigma_g \sim \text{HalfCauchy}(s_\sigma), \qquad \nu - 1 \sim \text{Exponential}(1/29)$$ Eq. 34 · BEST priors

BEST returns a joint posterior over $(\mu_{\text{treat}}, \mu_{\text{control}}, \sigma_{\text{treat}}, \sigma_{\text{control}}, \nu)$. From these we derive posteriors on the difference $\mu_{\text{treat}} - \mu_{\text{control}}$, on Cohen's $d$, on the variance ratio, and on any decision-relevant transformation. Three things make BEST strictly more informative than a Welch t-test:

  • Adaptive robustness via $\nu$. When the data have heavy tails, the posterior on $\nu$ concentrates near small values and outliers don't dominate inference. When the data are clean, $\nu$ grows large and the model approaches Gaussian. The model decides which regime applies; the analyst doesn't pre-commit.
  • Direct posterior probabilities. "$\Pr(\tau > 1.0 \mid \text{data}) = 0.83$" is a defensible answer to a defensible question. p-values are neither.
  • HDI + ROPE decisions. The 95% highest density interval (HDI) compared against a region of practical equivalence (ROPE) gives a richer 3-way decision than reject / fail-to-reject.

HDI + ROPE: decision-theoretic readouts

The 95% HDI is the narrowest interval containing 95% of posterior mass — the Bayesian counterpart to a confidence interval, but with the direct probabilistic interpretation that frequentist CIs lack. The ROPE is a domain-defined band around the null, e.g. $[-\$50\text{K/wk},\, +\$50\text{K/wk}]$ — within which an effect is "practically zero" for budget purposes. Combining the two yields a 3-way decision:

$$\text{Decision}\big(\mathrm{HDI}_{95},\, \mathrm{ROPE}\big) \;=\; \begin{cases} \text{accept } H_1 \;(\text{meaningful effect}) & \mathrm{HDI} \cap \mathrm{ROPE} = \varnothing \\[4pt] \text{accept } H_0 \;(\text{practical equivalence}) & \mathrm{HDI} \subset \mathrm{ROPE} \\[4pt] \text{undecided} \;(\text{collect more data}) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$ Eq. 35 · HDI + ROPE decision rule

The 3-state structure is materially more useful than the binary frequentist verdict. The "practical equivalence" conclusion — this channel's ROI is not meaningfully different from zero, divest — is a real budget decision that NHST can only reach via auxiliary equivalence tests (TOST). Under HDI+ROPE it is one of three primary outcomes.

Counterfactual forecasting: CausalImpact / BSTS

For geo-lift designs with one or a few treated geos and a sizeable donor pool, Brodersen et al.'s CausalImpact framework casts the analysis as a Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS) problem:

$$y_t \;=\; Z_t^{\top}\boldsymbol{\alpha}_t + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{t+1} \;=\; T_t\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}_t + R_t\,\boldsymbol{\eta}_t$$ Eq. 36 · BSTS state-space form

The state vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_t$ decomposes into local level, local trend, seasonality, and a spike-and-slab regression on donor-geo time series — the latter automatically performs variable selection across hundreds of potential control series. The model is fit on the pre-period; the post-period counterfactual is the posterior predictive forecast of $y_t$ conditioned on the donor evolution. The treatment effect is the posterior over (observed − forecast), available pointwise and cumulatively.

Two practical advantages over classical SCM:

  • Honest uncertainty propagation. Credible intervals on the cumulative effect reflect both observation noise and donor-mixture uncertainty. Permutation-based SCM intervals do not propagate the latter.
  • Spike-and-slab donor selection. No need for the simplex-restricted optimization in Eq. 27 — the model handles donor sparsity internally and reports posterior inclusion probabilities for each donor.

The reference implementation is the R CausalImpact package; Python equivalents (causalimpact, tfcausalimpact) and PyMC's structural time-series module are reasonable substitutes, though the original spike-and-slab implementation is hardest to match exactly.

Hierarchical / multilevel Bayesian models

When per-geo treatment effects vary, two extreme analytical options exist: pool everything into a single average effect (biased toward the mean, ignores heterogeneity), or estimate each geo separately (high-variance, no information sharing). The hierarchical Bayesian middle ground:

$$\tau_g \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(\bar{\tau},\, \sigma_\tau^2), \qquad \bar{\tau} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(0,\, s_{\bar\tau}^2), \qquad \sigma_\tau \;\sim\; \text{HalfNormal}(s_\sigma)$$ Eq. 37 · Hierarchical model for geo-level effects

Each geo's posterior treatment effect is shrunk toward the global mean by an amount that the data determine: noisy geos pull in further; geos with strong, consistent signals stay near their MLE. This is the Bayesian analogue of James–Stein shrinkage, and it dominates per-geo OLS in mean squared error whenever there are four or more groups (Stein 1956; Efron & Morris 1973).

For the calibration loop, the global ATE posterior $\bar\tau \mid \text{data}$ is what flows forward as the experiment-level estimate; the per-geo $\tau_g$ posteriors enable diagnostic inspection of heterogeneity — which markets are driving the effect, and whether the global summary is meaningful.

The PyMC specification is direct enough to write in a few lines:

# Hierarchical Bayesian DiD in PyMC
import pymc as pm

with pm.Model() as hier_did:
    # Population-level treatment effect (the calibration target)
    tau_bar   = pm.Normal("tau_bar", mu=0, sigma=1.0)
    sigma_tau = pm.HalfNormal("sigma_tau", sigma=0.3)

    # Geo-level effects, partially pooled toward tau_bar
    tau = pm.Normal("tau", mu=tau_bar, sigma=sigma_tau, shape=n_geos)

    # Geo + time fixed effects, observation noise
    alpha = pm.Normal("alpha", 0, 1.0, shape=n_geos)
    lam   = pm.Normal("lambda", 0, 1.0, shape=n_periods)
    sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=1.0)

    mu = alpha[geo_idx] + lam[time_idx] + tau[geo_idx] * D
    y  = pm.Normal("y", mu=mu, sigma=sigma, observed=Y)

    idata = pm.sample(2000, target_accept=0.95)

The posterior on tau_bar is what hands off to the calibration loop — analogous to $\hat{\beta}_k$ in the frequentist case, but as a full distribution rather than a point estimate plus SE.

Bayesian synthetic control

Several formulations exist; the most natural places a Dirichlet prior on the donor weights so the simplex constraint holds by construction:

$$\mathbf{w} \;\sim\; \text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}), \qquad y_{1,t} \;=\; \mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{y}_{\mathcal{D},t} + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,\sigma^2)$$ Eq. 38 · Bayesian SCM with Dirichlet weights

Two benefits over the optimized point-estimate SCM in Eq. 27. First, when the pre-period optimization is near-degenerate (multiple weight vectors fit equally well), the Dirichlet posterior reflects that ambiguity and the resulting counterfactual interval widens accordingly — rather than the optimization arbitrarily picking one solution and the analyst overstating precision. Second, donor-pool model averaging emerges naturally: the posterior over $\mathbf{w}$ assigns mass across plausible donor configurations, and the predictive counterfactual marginalizes over them.

Sequential Bayesian inference: no alpha-spending

Frequentist sequential testing is constrained by the multiple-comparisons problem across data looks. Each interim analysis costs Type-I error budget, and stopping rules must be planned in advance — O'Brien-Fleming, Pocock, or generic alpha-spending functions. Mid-test rule changes invalidate inference.

Bayesian sequential inference is structurally free of this constraint. Posteriors are valid at every time $t$ — there is no concept of "looks" because the posterior is conditional on the observed data, not on a hypothetical sampling distribution. A practical 3-state stopping rule:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{Stop with positive verdict} &\quad\text{when}\quad \Pr(\tau > \tau_{\min} \mid \text{data}_t) \ge p_{\text{high}} \\ \text{Stop with null verdict} &\quad\text{when}\quad \Pr(\tau \le 0 \mid \text{data}_t) \ge p_{\text{low}} \\ \text{Continue} &\quad\text{otherwise} \end{aligned}$$ Eq. 39 · Bayesian sequential stopping

With $p_{\text{high}} = p_{\text{low}} = 0.95$, this gives a clean 3-state evaluation at each point in the test window, with no penalty for looking and no requirement to pre-specify the look schedule. The cost of monitoring is computational, not statistical.

▸ Subtle but important

"Optional stopping doesn't bias Bayesian inference" is a real result, but it depends on the stopping rule being independent of the parameter of interest given the observed data. If the stopping rule is "stop when I get the answer I want," that violates the assumption — not via Bayesian-inference machinery, but via post-hoc reporting bias. Pre-register the stopping rule even in Bayesian settings; the gain over frequentist sequential testing is freedom from alpha-spending arithmetic, not freedom from honest reporting.

Adaptive allocation: Thompson sampling

When the experimental portfolio itself can be re-allocated during a calibration cycle — e.g., gradually shifting test budget across channels as posteriors update — Thompson sampling treats the allocation as a multi-armed bandit problem. The procedure at each round:

  1. Draw $\tilde\tau_k$ from the current posterior of each candidate channel $k$.
  2. Allocate the next test increment to $\arg\max_k \tilde\tau_k$.
  3. Observe outcomes and update posteriors via Bayes' rule.
  4. Repeat.

The procedure automatically balances exploration (testing channels with wider posteriors, since they are more likely to produce a maximal draw) against exploitation (concentrating on high-mean channels). For the EIG/EVOI portfolio, Thompson sampling is a natural drop-in for the greedy submodular allocation when budget is divisible mid-cycle. Convergence to the optimal allocation in stationary regimes is guaranteed under mild conditions (Russo et al. 2018).

Posterior predictive checks

A Bayesian model fit is not validated by a single goodness-of-fit number. The standard practice is the posterior predictive check: simulate replicated datasets $y^{\text{rep}}$ from the posterior predictive distribution, then compare summaries of $y^{\text{rep}}$ against the observed $y$. Deviations flag model misspecification — e.g., observed extremes that the model never produces in replication imply the likelihood family is too thin-tailed (a signal to switch from Gaussian to t).

For geo-lift specifically, the most useful PPCs are: (1) replicated pre-period geo trajectories vs. observed, to validate the donor-mixture / fixed-effects structure; (2) replicated post-period control-geo trajectories, to validate the counterfactual generation; and (3) replicated treatment-effect distributions, to validate the heterogeneity model. These run in standard PyMC / NumPyro / Stan tooling with a few extra lines.

When this complexity earns itself

Use these methods when…

  • Outcome data is heavy-tailed or known to contain real outliers (BEST).
  • Only one or a few geos are treated and the donor pool is rich (CausalImpact).
  • You expect or care about cross-geo heterogeneity (hierarchical).
  • You want a "practical equivalence" verdict, not just rejection (HDI+ROPE).
  • The test will be monitored continuously and stopped adaptively (sequential).
  • The team has PyMC/NumPyro fluency and tolerates 10–100× longer fitting times than OLS.

The bridge equation is sufficient when…

  • The frequentist estimator is well-specified and SE is approximately Gaussian.
  • Outcome data is well-behaved and outliers are rare or addressable separately.
  • The audience needs frequentist artifacts (p-values, pre-registration) for compliance.
  • The team is more comfortable defending a TWFE specification than a hierarchical prior.
  • Compute budget for retrospective analysis is tight.
▸ Closing principle

The bridge equation buys coherence between paradigms — frequentist outputs become Bayesian likelihoods, and the calibration loop continues. The fully Bayesian methods buy fidelity: when the data structure (heavy tails, single-treated geos, geo-level heterogeneity) or the design (sequential, adaptive) genuinely violates the Gaussian-likelihood approximation, native Bayesian methods give honest posteriors that the bridge cannot. For most production geo-lift readouts, the bridge is enough. For methodologically demanding cases — and for the upstream MMM itself — the methods above are the right primitives.

§ 01 · The pathology being addressed

Specification shopping is a structural problem, not a discipline problem

The OLS MMM workflow has degrees of freedom that must be resolved by some process. Today, that process is iterative and undocumented.

Throughout this guide the MMM has been treated as a black box that produces a posterior over channel ROIs. In production, the inside of that box is often a frequentist OLS regression that has been iterated on for weeks. The standard frequentist MMM workflow looks like this:

  1. Fit OLS with a default specification.
  2. Inspect coefficients. Some are wrong-signed; some are implausibly large; some controls "absorbed" media variation that should have been attributed.
  3. Adjust: change the adstock decay, log-transform the offending channels, add an interaction, drop a control, change the time window, group some channels together, winsorize outliers, swap the seasonality basis.
  4. Refit. Inspect again.
  5. Repeat steps 3–4 until the coefficients pass a "business sensibility" check.
  6. Report the chosen specification's coefficients and standard errors as if it had been the only model considered.

This is the garden of forking paths (Gelman & Loken 2013) applied to MMM. The reported standard errors are conditional on the chosen specification being the only one considered, while in fact the analyst tried fifty or a hundred specifications and selected the one they preferred. Reported confidence intervals systematically understate the true sampling-plus-specification uncertainty — and the inflation factor is rarely small.

$$\mathrm{Var}_{\text{true}}(\hat{\beta}) \;=\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}_{\text{sampling}}(\hat{\beta} \mid \text{spec})}_{\text{reported}} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}_{\text{specification}}(\hat{\beta})}_{\text{ignored}}$$ Eq. 40 · The variance the report is missing

In production MMMs, the specification-uncertainty term frequently dominates the sampling term — sometimes by an order of magnitude. The downstream effect is that the calibration loop, EIG/EVOI prioritization, and budget reallocation decisions are all conditioned on a prior that is far more confident than the data justify.

▸ Specific failure modes seen in production

Moulton bias from geo-duplicated national media. National-level media spend (broadcast TV, OOH, podcasts) is often duplicated across geos in a panel-data MMM. The within-geo variation of the duplicated regressor is exactly zero across geos, so the OLS estimator treats it as if there were $N_{\text{geo}} \times T$ independent observations of a regressor that has, in reality, only $T$ unique values. Standard errors collapse; reported significance is illusory. This is not fixed by cluster-robust SEs at the geo level — the regressor itself isn't varying at the geo level.

Post-hoc dummy variables. When a specific week or geo "doesn't fit," analysts add a dummy to absorb the residual. The remaining-coefficient SEs no longer reflect honest uncertainty about anything; the dummy was selected to make the rest of the fit look cleaner.

Selective transformation. If a channel's linear coefficient looks wrong, log-transform it. If a control's coefficient looks wrong, drop it. Each transformation choice is data-dependent; treating the final specification as if these choices were made a priori is a form of double-dipping.

Adstock and saturation grid search. When adstock decay or saturation half-saturation parameters are grid-searched and the best-fitting values plugged into a final OLS, the OLS standard errors don't reflect the uncertainty in those grid-search choices. A Bayesian formulation would propagate that uncertainty automatically.

What "specification" actually contains

The word "specification" hides a lot of degrees of freedom. A non-exhaustive list of choices that the analyst must make and that materially affect the estimated channel ROIs:

Structural choices

  • Variable inclusion/exclusion (which controls?)
  • Functional form per channel (linear / log / log-log)
  • Interactions between channels or with controls
  • Channel grouping (e.g., merging Display + Programmatic)
  • Time aggregation (daily / weekly / 4-weekly)
  • Geo aggregation (national / DMA / store-level)
  • Outcome transformation (raw / log / share)

Transformation choices

  • Adstock family (geometric / Weibull / delayed)
  • Adstock decay parameter values
  • Saturation family (Hill / log / negative-exponential)
  • Saturation parameter values
  • Outlier handling (drop / winsorize / cap)
  • Seasonality basis (Fourier order, holiday set)
  • Trend treatment (none / linear / spline)

Even with conservative ranges per choice, the cardinality of the specification space runs into the thousands. OLS picks one and reports its standard errors; the analyst's iterative selection process is silent.

§ 02 · A frequentist partial fix

Specification curve analysis

A diagnostic, not an estimator. Makes specification uncertainty visible without resolving it.

Simonsohn, Simmons & Nelson (2020) introduced specification curve analysis as a partial remedy. The procedure:

  1. Enumerate every reasonable specification (the "multiverse").
  2. Fit all of them.
  3. Plot the resulting coefficient estimates sorted by magnitude, with each spec's individual confidence interval.
  4. Report the distribution of estimates as the headline result, not any single one.

This makes specification uncertainty visible. Stakeholders can see that channel ROI ranges from 0.6 to 2.1 across plausible specifications, rather than being told "the model says 1.4 ± 0.1." It also breaks the cherry-picking incentive: if the curve is reported, there is no advantage to selecting the most flattering point.

What it doesn't do: provide proper inferential validity over the curve. The set of "reasonable specifications" is itself a judgement call, and the curve doesn't tell you which point on it to act on. It is a diagnostic, not an estimator.

§ 03 · A coherent alternative

Anatomy of a Bayesian MMM

A single generative model whose structural choices are made once and whose parameters are then jointly inferred.

A Bayesian MMM replaces the iterate-until-it-looks-right OLS workflow with a single generative model whose structural choices are made once and whose parameters are then jointly inferred. The minimal specification:

$$y_t \;=\; \beta_0 \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^K \beta_k \cdot S_k\!\big(\,A_k(\mathbf{x}_{k, 1:t};\, \alpha_k)\,;\, h_k, \kappa_k\big) \;+\; \mathbf{z}_t^{\top}\boldsymbol{\gamma} \;+\; \varepsilon_t$$ Eq. 41 · Bayesian MMM observation equation

where $\mathbf{x}_{k,1:t}$ is the spend history for channel $k$ up to time $t$, $A_k(\cdot; \alpha_k)$ is the adstock transformation with decay parameter $\alpha_k$, $S_k(\cdot; h_k, \kappa_k)$ is the saturation transformation with half-saturation $h_k$ and shape $\kappa_k$, $\beta_k$ is the channel coefficient, $\mathbf{z}_t$ are controls, and $\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$.

Crucially, the priors on $(\beta_k, \alpha_k, h_k, \kappa_k)$ are part of the specification — and they do explicitly the work that ad-hoc specification adjustments did implicitly. The standard set:

$$\beta_k \;\sim\; \text{HalfNormal}(s_\beta), \qquad \alpha_k \;\sim\; \text{Beta}(a_\alpha, b_\alpha), \qquad h_k \;\sim\; \text{Gamma}(a_h, b_h), \qquad \kappa_k \;\sim\; \text{Gamma}(a_\kappa, b_\kappa)$$ Eq. 42 · Standard prior families

A few principled prior choices replace a long list of ad-hoc decisions. The HalfNormal on $\beta_k$ enforces non-negativity (no more "wrong sign" channels to fix manually). The Beta on $\alpha_k$ enforces $[0,1]$ adstock decay. The Gamma priors on $h_k, \kappa_k$ keep saturation parameters in plausible ranges. None of these are absent from the OLS workflow — they were just being applied implicitly through specification iteration. Making them explicit is the entire point.

Hierarchical structure across channels

The next layer pools information across related channels — channels in the same category, sharing a buyer demographic, or sharing a creative team. The hierarchical specification:

$$\beta_k \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(\mu_{c(k)},\, \sigma_c^2), \qquad \mu_c \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}(\mu_0,\, \sigma_0^2), \qquad \sigma_c \;\sim\; \text{HalfNormal}(s_c)$$ Eq. 43 · Hierarchical channel structure

where $c(k)$ is the category index for channel $k$. A new low-data channel (just-launched podcast network, fresh affiliate program) inherits a sensible prior from its category-level mean and only deviates from it as data accumulate. The same partial-pooling principle from hierarchical DiD, applied upstream to the MMM coefficients themselves.

Adstock and saturation as parameterized priors

Adstock and saturation are usually the largest specification-shopping risk in OLS MMMs, because their parameters interact non-linearly with each other and with the channel coefficient. The Bayesian formulation exposes this directly. Geometric adstock with carryover $\alpha_k \in [0,1]$:

$$A_k(\mathbf{x}_{k,1:t};\, \alpha_k) \;=\; \sum_{\ell=0}^{L} \alpha_k^\ell \, x_{k,\, t-\ell}$$ Eq. 44 · Geometric adstock

Hill saturation with half-saturation $h_k$ and shape $\kappa_k$:

$$S_k(a;\, h_k, \kappa_k) \;=\; \frac{a^{\kappa_k}}{a^{\kappa_k} + h_k^{\kappa_k}}$$ Eq. 45 · Hill saturation

In the OLS workflow, $\alpha_k, h_k, \kappa_k$ are typically grid-searched, with the best-fitting values plugged into the final regression as if they had been known a priori. The reported OLS standard errors do not reflect uncertainty in those grid-search choices. In the Bayesian formulation they are inferred jointly with $\beta_k$, and the posterior on $\beta_k$ correctly marginalizes over the uncertainty in the transformation parameters.

▸ Why this matters for ROI

Channel ROI is not $\beta_k$ in isolation — it is a function of $\beta_k$, the saturation curve evaluated at the relevant spend level, and the adstock carryover. When all three are inferred jointly with proper uncertainty, the posterior on ROI has the right width. When the transformation parameters are point-estimated and only $\beta_k$ has a CI, the reported ROI uncertainty is artificially narrow. This is exactly the kind of overconfidence that propagates into bad budget decisions downstream.

The PyMC reference implementation

A working sketch — small enough to read end-to-end, complete enough to extend. Assumes weekly data, $K$ channels, and a controls matrix $\mathbf{Z}$:

# Bayesian MMM — minimal reference implementation
import pymc as pm
import pytensor.tensor as pt

def geometric_adstock(x, alpha, L=12):
    # Convolution with geometric kernel
    weights = alpha ** pt.arange(L)
    weights = weights / pt.sum(weights)
    return pt.batched_tensordot(x, weights, axes=1)

def hill_saturation(a, h, kappa):
    return a**kappa / (a**kappa + h**kappa)

with pm.Model() as mmm:
    # Channel-level coefficients with category hierarchy
    mu_cat    = pm.Normal("mu_cat", 0.5, 0.3, shape=n_cats)
    sigma_cat = pm.HalfNormal("sigma_cat", 0.2)
    beta      = pm.HalfNormal("beta", sigma=1.0, shape=n_channels)
    # Sign-constrained, hierarchically pooled across categories

    # Adstock and saturation with informative-but-honest priors
    alpha = pm.Beta("alpha", 2, 3, shape=n_channels)         # mean ≈ 0.4
    h     = pm.Gamma("h", alpha=2, beta=0.5, shape=n_channels)
    kappa = pm.Gamma("kappa", alpha=3, beta=2, shape=n_channels)

    # Channel contributions
    adstocked  = geometric_adstock(x_spend, alpha)
    saturated  = hill_saturation(adstocked, h[:, None], kappa[:, None])
    contrib    = beta[:, None] * saturated

    # Controls
    gamma = pm.Normal("gamma", 0, 1.0, shape=n_controls)

    # Observation equation
    mu      = pt.sum(contrib, axis=0) + Z @ gamma + intercept
    sigma_y = pm.HalfNormal("sigma_y", 0.5)
    y_obs   = pm.Normal("y_obs", mu=mu, sigma=sigma_y, observed=y)

    # Calibration prior from prior experiments (Eq. 20)
    for k in calibrated_channels:
        pm.Potential(f"cal_{k}",
            pm.logp(pm.Normal.dist(mu_cal[k], sigma_cal[k]),
                    g_k(beta[k], alpha[k], h[k], kappa[k])))

    idata = pm.sample(2000, target_accept=0.95, chains=4)

The calibration block at the bottom — the pm.Potential call — is what closes the loop with the calibration mechanisms in The Loop tab. Each calibrated channel gets a soft prior pulling the implied geo-lift effect toward the experimentally-measured value, with the experimental SE setting the strength of that pull.

§ 04 · Migration recipe

From OLS to Bayesian without breaking trust

A staged approach that minimizes risk and preserves stakeholder confidence.

The transition from a frequentist OLS-MMM-with-spec-shopping to a Bayesian MMM is rarely a single-day project. A staged approach that minimizes risk:

01

Audit the existing specification

Document every choice point in the current OLS workflow: variable list, transformations, adstock/saturation values, outlier handling, time/geo aggregation. Run a basic specification curve analysis (a few dozen plausible variants) to quantify the specification uncertainty in the existing setup. This is the baseline against which the Bayesian version will be evaluated.

02

Translate the linear model

Write the OLS specification as a generative model in PyMC or NumPyro. Replace any "wrong-sign-fixing" steps with sign-constrained priors (HalfNormal on media coefficients, free Normal on controls). Replace adstock and saturation grid searches with parameterized priors. The first fit should reproduce the OLS point estimates to within a few percent under weak priors; if it doesn't, the model is wrong, not the data.

03

Choose priors carefully

Priors need to be informative enough to regularize, weak enough to be defensible. Run prior predictive checks: simulate $y$ from the prior, confirm the simulated revenue trajectories are physically plausible (positive, in the right order of magnitude, no implausible spikes). If prior-predictive trajectories look wild, priors are too loose; if they look identical to the observed data without conditioning, priors are too tight.

04

Add hierarchical structure

Group channels by category (digital / broadcast / OOH / direct), brand subline, or audience. Add a category-level mean and variance per group. Inspect the posterior on $\sigma_c$ to see how much pooling the data support — if $\sigma_c$ is large, the categories aren't really informative and pooling does little; if small, the hierarchy is doing real work.

05

Validate

Posterior predictive checks (replicate the time series and compare to observed); cross-validation against a held-out time window (block CV, not random CV); convergence diagnostics ($\hat{R} < 1.01$, ESS > 400 per chain, no divergences). Compare predictive accuracy to the OLS baseline. The Bayesian version should match or beat OLS out-of-sample; if it doesn't, the priors or hierarchy are wrong.

06

Connect to the calibration loop

For each previously-experimented channel, add the soft-calibration term from Eq. 20 (or likelihood augmentation from Eq. 21). Refit. The posteriors on calibrated channels should now be tight around the experimentally-measured effects; uncalibrated channels keep their MMM-only width. The framework now operates on the Bayesian MMM directly.

07

Run both in parallel for one cycle

Don't decommission the OLS pipeline immediately. For one or two MMM cycles, produce both readouts and compare. Stakeholders need to see that the Bayesian version produces sensible answers under conditions they understand. Discrepancies should be diagnosed and explained, not hidden.

08

Decommission

Once the Bayesian version has demonstrated stability over two or three cycles and stakeholders are comfortable interpreting posterior outputs, retire the OLS pipeline. Maintain a documented audit trail of the migration so that the question "why did the model change" has a defensible answer.

What you gain, what you give up

What you gain

  • Honest uncertainty quantification — reported intervals correspond to actual coverage.
  • Sign and range constraints baked into priors, not into post-hoc selection.
  • Joint inference over coefficients, adstock, and saturation parameters.
  • Hierarchical pooling for low-data channels.
  • Direct integration with the calibration loop.
  • Posterior predictive checks as a principled model-criticism workflow.
  • Stakeholder-friendly probabilistic statements ("70% chance ROI exceeds 1.5").
  • An audit trail of priors and structural choices that survives team turnover.

What you give up

  • Compute time — MCMC runs are minutes to hours, not seconds.
  • Onboarding investment — the team needs PyMC/NumPyro fluency.
  • Prior defensibility — every prior choice is now an artifact you must justify, in writing.
  • The escape hatch of "the model said it" — the model now says what your priors and likelihood say.
  • Some stakeholder comfort — frequentist outputs feel familiar; posteriors and HDIs require explanation.
  • Easy A/B comparison with vendor MMM tools that report OLS coefficients only.

Common pitfalls in migration

  • Priors too tight. If the prior is informative enough that the data barely move it, you have replaced OLS overconfidence with prior overconfidence. Run sensitivity analyses; the posterior should respond meaningfully to the data over the range of plausible priors.
  • Priors too loose. If the prior is so weak that the posterior reproduces OLS exactly — including all the wrong-sign and implausibly-large issues — you have not done any of the regularization work. The HalfNormal scale and Beta hyperparameters need real thought.
  • Treating the first fit as final. A Bayesian MMM is iterated like any other model — but the iterations are over priors and structural choices, with everything pre-registered. Iterating until the posterior "looks right" is just spec shopping with extra steps.
  • Ignoring divergences. NUTS divergences indicate the geometry of the posterior is poorly conditioned — almost always due to a non-centered hierarchical parameter that should be reparameterized, or a prior that conflicts with the likelihood. Don't ignore them; they invalidate inference.
  • Skipping posterior predictive checks. Convergence diagnostics tell you the sampler is exploring the posterior; PPCs tell you the posterior corresponds to a believable data-generating process. Both are required; neither substitutes for the other.
  • Calibrating before validating. Don't add experimental calibration terms until the unconstrained Bayesian MMM passes its own validation. Adding calibration to a poorly-fitting model just hides the misfit behind tighter priors.
▸ Closing principle

Specification shopping is not an analyst-discipline problem. It is a structural feature of the OLS workflow — degrees of freedom that must be resolved by some process, and in OLS that process is iterative, undocumented, and outside the inferential framework. A Bayesian MMM moves those degrees of freedom inside the model, where they are subject to priors, posterior inference, and audit. The framework throughout this guide — EIG, EVOI, calibration, sequential design — assumes a coherent posterior over channel parameters as input. The Bayesian MMM is what produces that input honestly.

§ 08 · Anti-patterns & failure modes

Things that look like the framework but aren't

Six failure modes seen in similar programs. Calling them out up front so we recognize them when they happen.

  • "Run all the experiments we can think of." Submodularity says marginal information per experiment falls fast. Running 8 experiments to learn what 3 would have taught you is wasted budget. The priority engine is supposed to cap the program; treating it as an unconstrained idea-generator defeats the point.
  • "Calibrate every channel, every cycle." Calibration has a shelf life and a cost. Re-experimenting on a channel whose last test is 4 months old and stable is just lighting test budget on fire. Use the re-experimentation trigger; it exists for this reason.
  • "Use a flat prior so we're not biasing the answer." A flat prior reproduces the OLS pathology. Priors are how you regularize; refusing to use informative priors is a refusal to do the regularization work. Defensible > flat.
  • "The Bayesian MMM is wider than OLS, so it's worse." The Bayesian MMM is wider because the OLS interval was lying about its precision. Width is information, not weakness. The reporting templates need to make this distinction explicit.
  • "Let's just plug the experimental point estimate in as a fixed parameter." Hard calibration by point-substitution discards the experimental SE and produces over-confidence in the calibrated channel. Use the soft-calibration prior (Mechanism 1) or the augmented likelihood (Mechanism 2). Never the point.
  • "The model said it; I just report what comes out." The Bayesian MMM "says" what your priors and likelihood structure say. The analyst owns those choices; reporting templates should attribute structural decisions to the analyst, not to "the model."
▸ The most insidious failure mode

Stakeholders accept the new framework, but quietly continue making allocation decisions on instinct, treating the priority map as a research artifact rather than a budget recommendation. This is invisible from the inside — the team produces the deliverables, gets compliments, and nothing gets used. Detection: at the second-cycle review, ask the sponsor to point at a specific budget reallocation that happened because of a calibrated posterior. If they can't, the program isn't actually running yet — only the appearance of it is.

§ 01 · Symbols

Notation used throughout

Channel-level parameters

θ_k
True causal ROI / elasticity parameter for channel $k$. The unknown the MMM and experiments are jointly estimating.
μ_k, σ_k²
MMM posterior mean and variance for channel $k$ — the prior we bring into experimental planning.
τ_k, τ̂_k
True experimental treatment effect ($\tau_k$) and its noisy estimate ($\hat{\tau}_k$); $\tau_k = \theta_k$ when scales align.
σ_exp,k²
Variance of the experimental estimate for channel $k$ under design $d$. Function of $n_{\text{geos}}, T, \Delta_{\text{spend}}, s^2_{\text{geo}}$.
b_k, s_k
Budget allocation and spend share for channel $k$; $s_k = b_k / \sum_j b_j$.

Decision-theoretic objects

EIG(k, d)
Expected information gain — expected reduction in posterior entropy from running experiment $(k, d)$. Units: bits or nats.
EVOI(k)
Expected value of information — expected dollar improvement in budget-decision utility from learning $\hat{y}_k$. Units: currency.
EVPI(k)
Expected value of perfect information — upper bound on EVOI; assumes the experiment reveals $\theta_k$ exactly.
λ, γ, ρ
Composite-score weights: epistemic-vs-instrumental tradeoff ($\lambda$), cost penalty ($\gamma$), risk penalty ($\rho$).

Estimator objects

$\hat{\beta}$, SE
Frequentist point estimate and standard error from a TWFE / SCM / ASC analysis. Plug into Eq. 32 as a Gaussian likelihood.
HDI
Highest density interval — narrowest interval containing a specified posterior mass (typically 95%). Bayesian counterpart to a confidence interval, with direct probabilistic interpretation.
ROPE
Region of practical equivalence — a domain-defined band around the null within which an effect is considered "practically zero" for decision purposes.
ν (BEST)
Degrees-of-freedom parameter of the Student-t likelihood in BEST. Small ν → heavy tails / robust to outliers; large ν → approaches Gaussian.
§ 02 · Concepts

Working vocabulary

Bayesian inference

Posterior
Updated belief distribution after seeing data. The "answer" of a Bayesian analysis.
Prior
Initial belief distribution before seeing the data in question. Can encode domain knowledge or be deliberately weak.
Likelihood
Probability of observed data as a function of unknown parameters. The "data-generating model."
Conjugate prior
A prior whose form is preserved by Bayesian updating. Gaussian-Gaussian and Beta-Binomial are the most common.
KL divergence
Information-theoretic measure of how much one distribution differs from another. Asymmetric, non-negative, zero only when distributions match.
Mutual information
Reduction in entropy of one variable from observing another. EIG is mutual information between parameter and experimental outcome.
Submodularity
The "diminishing returns" property of set functions. Guarantees greedy algorithms achieve $(1-1/e) \approx 63\%$ of optimal.
Partial pooling
Hierarchical Bayesian shrinkage: per-group estimates pulled toward the population mean by a data-determined amount. Dominates per-group OLS in MSE.

MMM-specific

Geo-lift / MMT
Geographic experiments that randomize spend changes across markets to identify causal channel effects.
Calibration
Anchoring an MMM's parameters to experimentally-identified estimates — soft (priors), hard (constraints), or hybrid.
TWFE
Two-way fixed effects — DiD specification with separate geo and time intercepts. Requires cluster-robust SEs at the geo level.
BEST
Bayesian Estimation Supersedes the t-Test (Kruschke 2013) — robust two-group comparison via Student-t likelihood and HDI+ROPE decisions.
BSTS / CausalImpact
Bayesian Structural Time Series — state-space counterfactual forecasting for single-treated-unit geo-lifts. Brodersen et al.
Thompson sampling
Adaptive allocation rule: at each step, sample from posteriors and allocate to argmax. Automatically balances exploration and exploitation.
Specification shopping
Iterative selection of OLS specifications until coefficients pass an informal sensibility check, with reported standard errors conditioning only on the chosen specification — a form of garden-of-forking-paths p-hacking.
Specification curve
Diagnostic procedure: enumerate all reasonable specifications, fit them all, plot the distribution of estimates. Makes specification uncertainty visible without resolving it.
Adstock
Carry-over transformation modeling delayed media response. Geometric: $A_t = \sum_\ell \alpha^\ell x_{t-\ell}$. Treated as parameterized with prior on $\alpha$ in Bayesian MMM.
Saturation
Diminishing-returns transformation. Hill: $S(a) = a^\kappa / (a^\kappa + h^\kappa)$. Treated as parameterized with priors on $h, \kappa$ in Bayesian MMM.
Moulton bias
Variance underestimation that arises when a higher-aggregation regressor (e.g., national-level media) is duplicated across lower-level units (e.g., geos) in panel data.
