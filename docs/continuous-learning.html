Continuous learning — measuring your way to a plan

A self-contained loop that learns how spend drives outcome directly from designed experiments — one wave at a time, carrying its uncertainty forward — and turns that learning into a budget you can defend. No pre-fit marketing-mix model required.

Most planning starts from a model fit to observational history and hopes past spend was exogenous enough to be causal. Continuous learning takes the opposite route. It treats media planning as a sequential experiment: run a small, designed batch of geo tests, fit a response surface to what they measured, decide where the next dollar and the next test should go, and repeat — stopping when another round of testing no longer pays for itself.

Because every wave is experimentally assigned, the answer is causal by construction, not by assumption. And because the loop is Bayesian, the plan it produces arrives with its uncertainty attached: a credible band on the recommended split, a funding line that says which channels clear break-even and with what probability, and an explicit rule for when to stop testing and act.

What this is

A model-free geo response-surface bandit (mmm_framework.continuous_learning): a lightweight Bayesian surface — Hill saturation plus sign-informed channel synergies — fit from designed geo-week experiments, wrapped in a decision loop that allocates budget, prices the next experiment, and knows when to quit. It complements, rather than replaces, the model-anchored planner that sits on top of a fitted BayesianMMM.

The learning cycle

Each pass is a wave: a designed batch of geo cells run for a fixed window. The posterior is carried across waves by refitting on all the data collected so far, so every wave borrows strength from the last.

1 · Fit

Learn the surface

Fit a Bayesian response surface to the accumulated experiments. The output is a full posterior — a distribution over how each channel converts spend to outcome, not a single curve.

2 · Score & pick

Value the next test

Score candidate next experiments by how much they would improve the budget decision (value of information), and probe where the model is least sure — not just where the mean looks best.

3 · Run

Run a designed wave

Assign geos to a central-composite design around the current recommendation and hold them for a fixed window. Designed cross-sectional variation is what makes the readout causal.

4 · Update & decide

Refit, then stop or continue

Fold the readout in, refit, and re-price. When the expected value of another wave falls below its cost (the ENBS rule), the loop halts and hands you the plan.

💡 Model-free, but not evidence-free

“No model” means no pre-fit observational MMM is required — the loop learns its own surface from experiments. If you do have a fitted BayesianMMM, the model-anchored planner (the calibration loop) prioritizes experiments from that model instead. The two share conventions and are meant to be used with whatever evidence you have.

How it works · the response surface

This section is the intuition; for the full derivation — the expected-information-gain objective, the Gaussian/Laplace acquisition, D- and Ds-optimality, the knowledge gradient, and the ENBS stopping rule, each mapped to the code — see the mathematical foundations companion.

Everything rests on one differentiable function used in three places — the likelihood that fits it, the simulator that generates from it, and the allocator that optimizes it — so the optimizer can never disagree with the fitted model. Per geo-week, the incremental outcome of a scaled spend vector is the sum of each channel's saturating response plus a sign-informed interaction block:

\[ R(s) = \sum_c \beta_c\, f_c(s_c) \;+\; \sum_{c \,<\, c'} \gamma_{cc'}\, f_c(s_c)\, f_{c'}(s_{c'}) \] (1)

Here \( \beta_c \) is the channel ceiling and \( \gamma_{cc'} \) the synergy between a pair of channels — positive for complementarity, negative for cannibalization (heavily overlapping audiences). The object that matters is the per-channel activation \( f_c(\cdot) \): the method needs only a smooth, monotonically increasing, saturating curve with \( f_c(0) = 0 \) and values in \( [0, 1) \) — not one specific functional form. The single hard requirement is that it be differentiable, because the allocator optimizes budget by following the marginal response \( \partial R / \partial s_c \); the activation just needs a well-defined gradient everywhere.

The loop's default choice is a Hill curve, which gives each channel its own onset, steepness, and half-saturation:

\[ f_c(s_c) = \frac{s_c^{\alpha_c}}{\kappa_c^{\alpha_c} + s_c^{\alpha_c}} \] (2)

with \( \kappa_c \) the half-saturation (where the curve reaches half its ceiling) and \( \alpha_c \) the shape. This is convention-identical to the framework's SaturationType.HILL, so a continuous-learning posterior is directly comparable to a full MMM fit on the same channel. But the choice is genuinely pluggable: the loop ships a logistic activation \( f(s) = 1 - e^{-\lambda s} \) (a concave, no-inflection curve) that you select with one flag (activation="logistic") — the fit, the design, the planning, and the stopping rule are all indifferent to which family you pick, and a Michaelis–Menten or tanh curve would drop in the same way.

Priors inform; the experiment decides

The priors are weakly informative — non-negative ceilings, half-saturations of order one in scaled units, and sign-informed synergy priors that encode which channel pairs are expected to compete or complement. But the surface is identified by the designed experiment, not the prior: the signs of the synergies recover robustly, while their magnitudes stay prior-sensitive and should be treated as sign-reliable, magnitude-assumed until a wave probes them directly.

Designed experiments · the waves

A model is causal only because spend is experimentally assigned. Each wave uses a central-composite design around the current operating point: a centre cell, axial cells that move one channel at a time (the gradient), off-axis cells that move two channels together (the only way to see a synergy), and shut-off cells that set a channel to zero (which break the confound between a channel's main effect and its interactions).

Identification is non-negotiable, and the data contract builds it in:

  • a pre-period where every geo runs the status-quo allocation — this pins each geo's baseline and breaks the within-geo collinearity between baseline and incremental response; then
  • a test-period of designed cross-sectional variation (the central-composite cells, assigned across geos) — this is the variation that identifies the surface.

⚠️ No designed variation, no causal answer

Fit the same surface to observational spend with no pre/test split and no designed variation and the loop degenerates to an ordinary MMM with the usual confounding — the priors carry everything and the output is not causal. The pre-period and the designed cells are what make the readout an experiment rather than a correlation.

Planning under uncertainty

The decision each wave is: given today's beliefs, how should the budget be split, and is that split worth acting on yet? Three readouts answer it, and all three are distributions, not point numbers.

A posterior over the optimal split

Rather than optimize the average curve, the planner solves the allocation problem under each posterior draw — Thompson sampling. The result is a distribution over the optimal split: its mean is the recommendation, and its spread is the exploration signal that tells you which channels you are still unsure how to fund. The response surface is non-concave (negative synergies create ridges), so the allocator multi-starts and keeps the best.

The funding line

A channel is funded where the posterior says its marginal return clears break-even — where \( P(\text{value} \cdot \partial R / \partial s_c \,>\, 1) \,>\, 0.5 \). The funding line is the decision readout each wave: it names the channels worth another dollar, each with the probability that they are, so a channel funded “70% likely above the line” is treated very differently from one at 95%.

Explore versus exploit

Where should the next test go? Not necessarily at the greedy peak. The acquisition surface adds an exploration bonus to the mean — an upper-confidence-bound style score — so the next experiment is pulled toward regions the model is least sure about. In the animation, the black diamond (acquisition) sits off the white star (greedy peak) precisely because the uncertainty there is high; as the surface tightens, the two converge.

The value of an experiment, in decision terms

Under the hood, candidate tests are scored by how much they would improve the budget decision (a value-of-information / knowledge-gradient calculation), which the framework can approximate cheaply with a Laplace update rather than a full refit per candidate. The point is that a test is worth running when what it teaches changes what you would do — not merely when it reduces a standard error.

Knowing when to stop

Continuous learning is only useful if it also knows when to stop learning. Each wave the loop computes the expected regret — the profit still on the table from posterior uncertainty, the gap between acting on today's consensus split and acting with perfect knowledge. Multiply that by the margin and the population it affects, subtract the cost of running a wave, and you have the expected net benefit of sampling:

\[ \text{ENBS} \;=\; \mathbb{E}[\text{regret}] \cdot \text{margin} \cdot \text{population} \;-\; \text{wave cost} \] (3)

While a channel's or a pair's share of the expected regret exceeds the cost of a wave that would resolve it, keep testing. Once no wave's expected reallocation value clears its cost — ENBS ≤ 0 — halt and allocate. The rule is what keeps the loop from testing forever or chasing noise; visually, it fires just as the acquisition point stops moving.

A harder problem

The clean world above converges quickly. Real media rarely does. The animation below runs the same loop on a deliberately harder world: the two slice channels strongly cannibalize each other — a large negative synergy, as if their audiences heavily overlap — so the response surface has a ridge. You cannot fund both; the optimum is a narrow trade-off, the channels' main effects are close together (hard to rank), and saturation is slow.

This is the honest picture of measurement under uncertainty. Learning is not a smooth march to a known answer; it is a sequence of noisy readouts that gradually rule out the wrong ridges. The loop's job is to keep each test cheap and pointed at what still matters, and to be candid — through the uncertainty surface and the funding line — about how much is still unknown.

Application to media planning under uncertainty

Why run planning this way? Because the numbers a plan rests on are rarely known, and pretending otherwise is where budgets go to waste.

It defuses demand-chasing

Channels that ride demand — that spend more precisely when sales are already rising — look fantastic in an observational model that can't separate cause from confound. Designed experiments break that back-door path directly, so the loop credits incremental effect, not coincidence.

It turns a big bet into small ones

Instead of committing a full budget on a single fitted curve, you commit a little, in a designed test, and let each readout de-risk the next allocation. The trust region moves toward the optimum as the evidence accrues.

It reports what it doesn't know

The recommendation is a posterior mean with a credible band; the funding line gives each channel a probability of clearing break-even. A planner can act on the confident channels and keep testing the uncertain ones — rather than treating every point estimate as fact.

It decides when to stop

The ENBS rule converts “should we test more?” from a gut call into arithmetic: keep testing while the profit still at risk from uncertainty outweighs the cost of a wave; otherwise allocate. No infinite testing, no chasing noise.

In practice this is a fit for the marketing organizations that already run geo experiments — dark markets, matched-market lift tests, holdouts — and want a principled way to sequence them toward a budget rather than running one-off tests and eyeballing the results. It pairs naturally with the model-anchored calibration loop: use the model-free bandit when you have little usable history, and fold experiment readouts back into a fitted MMM when you do.

💡 A plan is a decision under uncertainty, not a forecast

The deliverable is not “channel X returns 3.2×.” It is “shift budget toward X and Y; X clears break-even with high probability, Y is close but uncertain, and one more geo wave on Y would pay for itself.” The uncertainty is the product, not a caveat on it.

What it assumes

Every measurement method rests on assumptions; honesty means stating them.

  • Designed, randomized assignment. Geos must be assigned to cells (round-robin, or stratified on pre-period level/variance). Without designed variation the causal claim collapses.
  • A pinned baseline. The pre-period must share a common allocation so each geo's intercept is identified separately from its incremental response.
  • No cross-geo spillover. Treated geos are assumed not to contaminate control geos. Overlapping media markets violate this and must be handled in the assignment.
  • Structural stationarity. The response curve is assumed stable over the windows being compared; a channel whose effectiveness shifts mid-flight breaks the surface.
  • Non-randomizable channels stay honest. A walled-garden channel that can't be geo-co-randomized is demoted to a main-effect-only estimate, and its synergies are flagged as prior-driven, not measured.

⚠️ Sign-reliable, magnitude-assumed

The signs of synergies and the ordering of the strong channels recover robustly; the magnitudes of weakly-probed interactions track their priors. Never present a prior-dominated synergy magnitude as a measured result — the loop's own prior-sensitivity audit exists to catch exactly this.

Try it

The whole loop is a small Python API. A single designed wave, a fit, and the three decision readouts:

import numpy as np
import mmm_framework.continuous_learning as cl

# A synthetic world with known ground truth (swap for your own geo panel).
world = cl.make_world(seed=0)
center = np.full(4, 0.7)                       # current operating allocation (scaled)

# Run one designed central-composite wave, then fit the response surface.
data = cl.simulate_panel(world, center, n_geo=80, t_pre=6, t_test=10, noise=0.5)
post = cl.fit(data, channels=world.channels, pair_signs=cl.PAIR_SIGNS_EXAMPLE)

# Plan under uncertainty: recommendation, funding line, value still on the table.
rec = cl.recommend_allocation(post, B=3.2, value=5.0, mode="fixed")
mroas_mean, prob_above_line, _ = cl.marginal_roas(post, rec, value=5.0)
expected_regret, *_ = cl.expected_regret(post, B=3.2, value=5.0, mode="fixed")

Or drive the full sequential loop end-to-end — refit-on-all-data across waves, with the ENBS stopping rule — and read back the per-wave decision trace:

import mmm_framework.continuous_learning as cl

world = cl.make_world(seed=0)
trace = cl.run_closed_loop(
    world, center=[0.7, 0.7, 0.7, 0.7], B=3.2, value=5.0,
    margin=1.0, population=2.0, wave_cost=0.45, max_waves=4,
)
for wave in trace["history"]:
    print(wave["wave"], wave["e_regret"], wave["enbs"], wave["stop"])
