1 Define the Question

What do you actually want to learn?

Every good analysis starts with a clear question. This might seem obvious, but skipping this step is the most common mistake in marketing measurement. Without a clear question, you end up with lots of numbers but no insights.

Think of it like planning a trip

You wouldn't open Google Maps before knowing where you want to go. The destination determines the route. Similarly, your business question determines what analysis to run, what data you need, and how to interpret the results.

üí∞

üîÆ

"What happens if we change spend?"

Our Example Question

For this walkthrough: "What is the ROI of each marketing channel, and how should we allocate our budget?"

This is a causal question‚Äîwe want to know if changing our marketing spend will cause sales to change. That's harder than measuring correlation, but it's what we need for actual decisions.

Why we write this down before analyzing

When you decide your question after seeing the data, you can unconsciously twist the question to match whatever the data happened to show. That's like drawing a target around wherever your arrow landed‚Äîit looks impressive but doesn't mean you're a good archer.

This practice of deciding your analysis plan beforehand is called pre-registration, and it's one of the most important safeguards against fooling yourself.

2 Tell the Story of Your Data

What process generated these observations? Think causally.

Before touching any model code, write down the generative story‚Äîa narrative of how you believe your data came to be. This isn't just about marketing effects; it's about everything that affects sales, including what else might create spurious associations between media and outcomes.

üè†

Chapter 1: The Baseline

Even without any marketing, your brand would sell some products. People who already know and love you, people who stumble upon you in stores, repeat customers. This is your baseline demand‚Äîthe foundation everything else builds upon.

üìà

Chapter 2: The Underlying Trend

Is your category growing, stable, or declining? Your brand might be gaining share or losing it. This underlying trajectory exists independent of weekly marketing fluctuations. A new market entrant grows; a legacy brand in a dying category declines. The trend captures this long-term direction.

üåä

Chapter 3: Seasonal Rhythms

Sales don't flow evenly through the year. Ice cream peaks in summer, toys in December, fitness products in January. These seasonal patterns are predictable rhythms that repeat year after year, driven by weather, holidays, and cultural cycles‚Äînot by marketing.

üì∫

Chapter 4: Marketing Lifts Sales

Each channel (TV, digital, etc.) adds sales above baseline. But the first million dollars of TV spend helps more than the tenth million‚Äîdiminishing returns. And today's ad affects sales for several weeks‚Äîcarryover effects.

‚öñÔ∏è

Chapter 5: What Else Affects Sales?

The brand increased distribution to stave off the effects of an economic downturn and ran more media to support this push into new markets—confounder. Some competitors started to run a new campaign in Q3, which the brand matched with extra spend to maintain share of voice—confounder. Weather patterns influence consumer behavior, with warmer weeks boosting in store sales—precision control. We want to isolate the total effect of marketing on sales, so we will leave out brand awareness—mediator. The brand provided survey responses on customer satisfaction, but since this is influenced by sales (only people who buy are given the survey)—collider—we will exclude it to avoid bias.

Trend & Seasonality Are Not Redundant with Seasonal Controls

You might wonder: "If I include weather (which is seasonal) and gas prices (which have trends), do I still need explicit trend and seasonality components?" Yes, absolutely.

External factors like weather or gas prices capture specific mechanisms‚Äîhow temperature affects store visits, how fuel costs affect discretionary spending. Your model's trend and seasonality components capture something different: the overall rhythms of demand and long-term trajectory that aren't fully explained by any single external factor.

üå°Ô∏è
Weather captures...

The specific effect of temperature/precipitation on behavior (e.g., hot days ‚Üí more ice cream)

üåä
Seasonality captures...

The overall annual rhythm of demand (e.g., Q4 holiday surge) that persists even controlling for weather

Think of it this way: Even if you perfectly controlled for every external factor, would demand still have an annual pattern? Would the brand still be growing or declining? If yes, you need trend and seasonality components. These are confounders (they affect both media planning and sales), so they must always be included‚Äîregardless of what other seasonal variables you have.

üîç Deep Dive: Why Confounders Must Be Included

‚ñº

A confounder creates a spurious correlation between media and sales that has nothing to do with causation. If you don't control for it, your media effect estimate will absorb this spurious correlation and be biased.

The Ice Cream & Drowning Fallacy

Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are correlated. But ice cream doesn't cause drowning‚Äîsummer is the confounder that increases both. If you tried to estimate "the effect of ice cream on drowning" without controlling for season, you'd get a spuriously positive number.

In MMM, distribution works the same way: brands with more distribution spend more on media AND sell more. If you don't control for distribution, media looks more effective than it really is.

Distribution Example

Expanding to 1,000 new stores triggers both media spend increase AND sales increase.

If omitted: Media ROI inflated ‚Üë
Seasonality Example

Q4 holiday season drives both ad spending spikes AND natural demand surges.

If omitted: Holiday campaigns overcredited ‚Üë
Competitor Example

Competitor launches ‚Üí you increase media ‚Üí their launch hurts your sales.

If omitted: Your media looks ineffective ‚Üì

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Over-Interpret Confounder Coefficients

When you include a variable to control for confounding, its coefficient may not be interpretable as a causal effect. Distribution might show a negative coefficient because other omitted variables (market saturation, competitive intensity) are correlated with it. This is sometimes called the table 2 fallacy

The goal: Get unbiased media effects, not to understand distribution's causal impact. Include distribution to isolate media effects‚Äîdon't agonize over its sign.

üéØ Deep Dive: Precision Controls & Variable Selection

‚ñº

Precision controls only affect the outcome‚Äîthey're uncorrelated with your treatment (media spending). Including them reduces noise and gives you tighter credible intervals on media effects, but omitting them doesn't bias your estimates.

The Scale Analogy

Imagine weighing packages on a scale that wobbles. The wobble (weather, gas prices) adds noise to your measurements but doesn't systematically make packages heavier or lighter. Stabilizing the scale (including precision controls) gives more precise readings but doesn't change the average weight.

Because precision controls don't affect media spending, it's safe to apply Bayesian variable selection (horseshoe, spike-and-slab) to them. If they turn out to be irrelevant, we shrink them toward zero without biasing our media estimates.

‚úì The Key Test

Ask: "Does this variable influence how we allocate media budget?" If no, it's likely a precision control. If yes, it's probably a confounder and must be protected from variable selection.

üîó Deep Dive: Mediators‚ÄîHandle with Care

‚ñº

A mediator lies on the causal path between media and sales. Brand awareness is the classic example: TV advertising increases awareness, and awareness drives purchase. The chain is: Media ‚Üí Awareness ‚Üí Sales.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Blocking Problem

If you control for awareness in your model, you're asking: "What's the effect of media on sales, holding awareness constant?" But media works through awareness! You've just blocked the causal pathway and will dramatically underestimate media's true effect.

Rule: For the total effect of media on sales, exclude mediators. Only include mediators if you specifically want to decompose paths (direct vs. indirect effects).

Get full effect through all paths
‚ö†Ô∏è Include Awareness

"How much of TV's effect is through awareness vs. direct?"

üí• Deep Dive: Colliders‚ÄîThe Hidden Trap

‚ñº

A collider is caused by both media and sales. Unlike confounders (which we must control) and mediators (which we usually exclude), colliders are dangerous specifically when we control for them.

The Hollywood Example

Among Hollywood actors, there's a negative correlation between attractiveness and acting talent. Does being attractive make you worse at acting? No‚Äîit's selection bias. To become a Hollywood actor (the collider), you need either looks OR talent OR both. By conditioning on "is a Hollywood actor," you've created a spurious negative relationship.

In MMM, "survey respondent" can be a collider: people who both saw your ads AND bought your product are more likely to respond to a brand survey. Controlling for survey response creates a spurious association between ad exposure and purchase.

üö´ Never Control for Colliders

Common colliders in marketing data include: survey response, website visit + purchase combinations, and any "engagement metric" that's caused by both advertising and underlying purchase intent. When in doubt, draw the DAG and check the arrow directions.

‚úì Correct Model

With distribution included, we isolate the true causal effect of media (0.25). The correlation between media and sales that was actually due to distribution is properly attributed‚Äînot falsely credited to media.

üé≤

Chapter 6: Random Variation

Even after accounting for everything‚Äîbaseline, trend, seasonality, marketing, and control factors‚Äîthere's always unexplained variation. A celebrity mentions your product, a competitor has a recall, an unexpected weather event. We can't predict these, but we acknowledge they exist as the error term.

üìñ Putting the Story Together

"Sales start from a baseline of existing demand, following an underlying trend (growth or decline). This foundation rises and falls with seasonal patterns. Marketing channels add to this, but with diminishing returns as spend increases, and effects carry over for several weeks. Meanwhile, other factors‚Äîdistribution, pricing, competition‚Äîaffect both media decisions and sales, creating spurious correlations we must control for. Weather and other external events which effect sales but do not impact media spend can help isolate the media effect, but if excluded won't bias results. We avoid controlling for variables that lie on the causal path from media to sales (like brand awareness) to get the total effect of marketing, unless we specifically want to decompose paths. And we never control for colliders like survey response, which would introduce bias. Finally, even after accounting for all these factors, some variation in sales remains unexplained. "

This story‚Äîthese assumptions‚Äîwill become our mathematical model. If we're wrong about any part, especially the causal structure, our results will be wrong. That's why we classify variables before seeing results, not after.

4 Prior Predictive Check

Do our assumptions generate plausible predictions?

Before showing the model any real data, we test whether our assumptions could plausibly generate realistic-looking outcomes. This catches problems early‚Äîbefore we waste time fitting a broken model.

Like a dress rehearsal

Before opening night, actors do a full rehearsal to catch problems. We run our model "forward" without real data: given our assumptions about how marketing works, what kind of sales numbers would we expect to see?

5 Fit the Model

Let the data update our beliefs

Now we show our model the actual sales data and let it figure out what the media effects probably are. This is the core of Bayesian inference: starting with prior beliefs, updating them with evidence.

Updating opinions with evidence

Imagine you think a coin is fair. Then you flip it 100 times and get 75 heads. You'd update your belief‚Äîmaybe it's biased. Our model does the same: it starts with prior beliefs about media effects and updates them based on the sales data.
