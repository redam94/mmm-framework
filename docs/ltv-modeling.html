LTV Modeling

A channel that acquires loyal, high-spending customers and a channel that acquires one-time bargain hunters can look identical on cost-per-acquisition. They are not identical businesses. This page covers the framework's Bayesian customer-lifetime-value model — how it turns a raw transaction log into per-customer and per-acquisition-channel value with full posterior uncertainty, and how that value flows back into experiments, acquisition economics, and budget decisions.

Why lifetime value

Most marketing measurement stops at the first conversion: an experiment reads out incremental sign-ups, an MMM attributes first purchases. But the money is in what happens afterwards — repeat purchases, retention, spend per order. Valuing every conversion at the same short-term number systematically over-funds channels that acquire cheap, low-quality customers and under-funds channels that acquire durable ones.

The fix is to model the customer, not just the conversion. The framework's bayesian_clv garden model estimates, for every customer:

  • How many more purchases they will make over a horizon,
  • What each future purchase is worth,
  • Whether they are still "alive" (haven't silently churned),

and rolls those into a discounted customer lifetime value — as a posterior distribution, not a point guess. Because it is a Model Garden family, it rides the same fit / estimand / report / serialization pipeline as every other model: no bespoke infrastructure.

The model — BG/NBD + Gamma-Gamma

The engine is the classic non-contractual "buy-till-you-die" pair (Fader–Hardie–Lee 2005; Fader–Hardie 2013), made fully Bayesian:

  • BG/NBD purchase dynamics. Each customer purchases at a latent Poisson rate \( \lambda_i \sim \text{Gamma}(r, \alpha) \) and, after every purchase, drops out for good with probability \( p_i \sim \text{Beta}(a, b) \). Integrating the latents gives a closed-form likelihood in three per-customer statistics — frequency, recency, and age — so the graph has a handful of population parameters regardless of how many customers you have.
  • Gamma-Gamma monetary value. Transaction values are \( z \sim \text{Gamma}(p, \nu_i) \) with a per-customer spend scale \( \nu_i \sim \text{Gamma}(q, \gamma) \), independent of purchase frequency (a checkable assumption). One-time buyers carry no repeat-value signal and shrink to the population mean.

Per-customer quantities — \( P(\text{alive}) \), expected purchases over the horizon (via the Gaussian hypergeometric conditional-expectation formula), expected value per purchase, and their product, CLV — are deterministic functions of the population posterior, so every one of them arrives with a credible interval.

Why Bayesian rather than the usual MLE fit? Because the numbers feed decisions. A posterior on CLV flows into the estimand engine, the report, and the experiment-economics machinery exactly like an MMM's ROI does — including honest uncertainty on "is Search's CLV really higher than Social's?"

From transactions to RFM

The model conditions on four per-customer sufficient statistics, computed from a raw transaction log over a calibration window: 

from mmm_framework.ltv import transactions_to_rfm

rfm = transactions_to_rfm(
    transactions,                      # one row per purchase
    customer_col="customer_id",
    date_col="date",
    value_col="value",                 # enables the monetary block
    observation_end="2025-06-30",      # calibration cutoff
    freq="W",                          # recency / age measured in weeks
    segment_col="acquisition_channel", # carried through for segment CLV
)
# → one row per customer: frequency, recency, T, monetary, n_txn, segment

Conventions that matter: frequency counts repeat purchases (the acquisition purchase itself carries no rate information under the model); monetary is the mean of repeat values only (NaN for one-time buyers); same-day duplicates merge into one purchase event; and the segment is the first transaction's attribute — acquisition attribution. In chat, the agent tool build_rfm_from_transactions performs this step on the loaded dataset and makes the RFM table the working dataset.

Fitting BayesianCLV

BayesianCLV lives in the Model Garden as a non-MMM family (kind = "clv" — no channels, no spend; each observation is a customer). Its settable configuration covers the horizon, discounting, the monetary toggle, and prior scales: 

# The example garden model (examples/garden_models/bayesian_clv.py)
from bayesian_clv import BayesianCLV, rfm_panel, segment_model_params
from mmm_framework.config import ModelConfig
from mmm_framework.model import TrendConfig
from mmm_framework.model.trend_config import TrendType

clv_model = BayesianCLV(
    rfm_panel(rfm),                    # wraps the RFM frame as a panel
    ModelConfig(),
    TrendConfig(type=TrendType.NONE),
    model_params={
        "horizon_periods": 52,         # CLV horizon (weeks)
        "discount_rate_annual": 0.10,
        **segment_model_params(rfm),   # per-channel segment CLV (below)
    },
)
clv_model.fit(method="map")            # seconds; or full NUTS for final numbers
clv_model.customer_value_summary()     # mean/total CLV, P(alive), CLV deciles

Because the per-customer latents are integrated out analytically, a MAP fit takes seconds even on thousands of customers, and NUTS is comfortably tractable for final, fully-calibrated numbers. The declared estimands — mean_clv, total_clv, mean_expected_purchases, mean_p_alive — surface through the standard estimand engine, and the report renders a customer-value table via the latent-structure section.

Segment CLV by acquisition channel

The decision-grade question is rarely "what is CLV?" — it is "whose customers are worth more?" With a segment column configured, the model adds a segment_clv_<channel> posterior per acquisition channel: 

clv_model.segment_clv_means()
# → {"Search": 37.8, "Social": 14.7}   (posterior means; full HDIs in the summary)

On synthetic ground truth where Search is planted to acquire genuinely higher-value customers, the fitted segment CLV recovers the planted ordering decisively — that recovery is a pinned regression test, not a hope.

CLV-valued experiments

An acquisition experiment measured in conversions should value each conversion at the lifetime value of the customer it brings in. The experiment toolkit's ghost-ads power calculator accepts the fitted model's CLV directly — in chat, ghost_ads_power_calc(value_from_clv=True, clv_segment="Search") pulls the segment's posterior CLV as value_per_conversion, so the economic break-even lift reflects lifetime worth. For the geo/switchback methods and the net-economics headline (reallocation gain vs test loss), the same bridge is a usage pattern: pass the CLV as the margin per conversion-KPI.

CLV vs CAC — blended acquisition economics

from mmm_framework.ltv import clv_to_cac

clv_to_cac(
    {"Search": 37.8, "Social": 14.7},      # posterior segment CLV
    {"Search": 25.0, "Social": 12.0},      # CAC = spend / new customers
)
#            clv   cac  clv_minus_cac  clv_to_cac
# Search    37.8  25.0           12.8        1.51
# Social    14.7  12.0            2.7        1.22

Search costs twice as much per acquisition — and still wins on net value, because its customers are worth 2.6× as much over their lifetime. That inversion is invisible to a CPA dashboard. CAC is deliberately an input here (spend ÷ new customers from your channel reporting): the CLV model never sees spend, so the economics stay an explicit, auditable join rather than a hidden modeling assumption.

Cohort CLV as a KPI

To point the whole measurement loop at lifetime value, collapse acquisitions into a weekly cohort-value series and use it as the KPI an MMM or experiment targets: 

from mmm_framework.ltv import new_customer_clv_series

series = new_customer_clv_series(transactions, clv_per_customer)
# per week: new_customers, cohort_clv, mean_clv, n_unvalued
# → feed `cohort_clv` to an MMM as a monetary KPI (margin_per_kpi = 1)

Media is then credited with the lifetime value of the customers it acquired that week — closing the chain spend → acquisitions → lifetime value. The n_unvalued column keeps the join honest: customers in the log but missing from the CLV index are counted but contribute zero value, visibly.

Validation on known truth

The model is tested the way everything in this framework is tested — against synthetic worlds with a known answer key (mmm_framework.synth.dgp_clv.make_clv_world): a simulator that follows the exact BG/NBD + Gamma-Gamma generative story, with planted population parameters, optional per-channel heterogeneity, and a calibration/holdout split.

  • Parameter recovery — MAP estimates land on the planted \( r, \alpha, a, b, p, q, \gamma \) (e.g. \(r\): 0.84 vs true 0.80).
  • Holdout prediction — predicted purchases over the holdout window match actuals in aggregate and rank customers correctly.
  • Segment ordering — planted high-value channels recover as high-CLV segments.
A subtlety the tests pinned: in the BG/NBD story the dropout coin is flipped after every repeat purchase — not after the acquisition purchase. A simulator (or a mental model) that lets customers die at acquisition over-produces one-time buyers and biases the purchase-rate parameters low by roughly 2×. If your intuition about "expected repeats" disagrees with the model, check this convention first.

Honest caveats

  • Non-contractual assumption. BG/NBD models silent churn in always-on purchasing. Subscription businesses (observed churn) want a survival/retention model instead — the garden contract accepts one as its own family.
  • Stationarity. CLV projections assume the fitted purchase/dropout/value process persists over the horizon. Pricing changes or product overhauls break that; refit after regime changes.
  • Frequency–value independence. Gamma-Gamma assumes spend per order is independent of order frequency. Check the empirical correlation; if it is materially non-zero, treat monetary results with caution.
  • Segment CLV is descriptive, not causal. "Search customers are worth more" does not mean "more Search spend creates high-value customers" — that claim needs an experiment, which is exactly what the CLV-valued experiment bridge is for.
